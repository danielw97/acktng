# Lore: The Sky-Road Recognition Dispute

## What Recognition Means

The Serathi sky-roads are not metaphor. They are transit infrastructure: defined corridors of air above specific terrain features, navigated by precise sky-line reference, maintained through generational knowledge accumulated in roost-chronicles, and governed by the Sky-Road Compact's territorial protocols. The Serathi do not claim the land beneath them. They claim the air above it — or more precisely, they claim the right to govern transit through that air by the same legal framework that governs transit on the ground roads below.

**Recognition**, in the formal sense the Serathi have sought from multiple human institutions, would mean three things:

First, that sky-road corridors constitute infrastructure with recognized legal status, not merely aerial routes that exist at the tolerance of ground-based governance. Recognized infrastructure can be entered into road-law agreements, compensation negotiations, and maintenance obligation frameworks. Unrecognized routes can be overflown, blocked, or disrupted by ground-based activity without legal remedy.

Second, that the Sky-Road Compact's territorial protocols have standing in human corridor law — that disputes about sky-road access can be adjudicated by corridor-law mechanisms rather than requiring the Serathi to establish harm to their physical persons before any legal remedy applies.

Third, and most practically, that the ground-level expansion of human infrastructure — road widening, new construction on ridge-lines, the installation of signal towers and commercial cable-lines that cross corridor airspace — requires consultation with and compensation to the sky-road communities affected, in the same way that ground-road construction requires negotiation with landholders in the path of expansion.

None of these three things has been formally granted by any major human institution. Kiess has granted the first, provisionally, for one corridor segment. Midgaard has granted none of them. Kowloon has not been formally petitioned, because the Serathi sky-roads do not extensively cross Kowloon's approach territory, though this may be changing as Kowloon's commercial network expands northward.

## The History of the Negotiation

### The Kiess Relationship: Evermeet's Record as Leverage

Kiess and the Serathi have the longest contact history of any human city and sky-road community. The roost-chronicles of the western sky-readers include continuous observation records of the Evermeet civilization that preceded Kiess, the collapse event that Serathi sky-readers documented from above, and the gradual establishment of Kiess on the ruins that followed. The Serathi understanding of Evermeet's collapse — that it consumed itself through institutional overreach rather than falling to external force — is documented in chronicle language that the Kiess Syndic Council recognizes as potentially politically significant.

The negotiation between Kiess and the Serathi sky-readers over the Evermeet chronicles has been ongoing for three generations. The Syndic Council wants access to the chronicles. The sky-readers have offered recitation of relevant sections — they do not provide physical copies — in exchange for formal recognition of specific corridor segments that Kiess's expanding overland infrastructure has encroached upon.

Two generations ago, a Syndic Council session reached a partial agreement: the sky-readers recited the Evermeet collapse accounts for a Council session, and the Council issued a provisional recognition order for the Thornwall Corridor — the sky-road segment above the ridge-line most directly affected by Kiess's western expansion infrastructure. The recognition is provisional because the Syndic Council's legal staff argued, correctly, that the Council lacked unilateral authority to create new legal categories for aerial infrastructure without inter-city corridor law amendment. The provisional recognition has been in force for two generations without advancing to formal status, because inter-city corridor law amendment requires Midgaard's participation and Midgaard has declined to engage with the question.

The sky-readers consider the provisional recognition a useful precedent rather than a resolution. They continue to offer Evermeet chronicle recitations in exchange for further recognition steps. The Syndic Council considers the relationship valuable. The negotiation continues.

### The Midgaard Impasse

Midgaard's refusal to engage with sky-road recognition is the central structural obstacle in the dispute. Corridor road law is governed from Midgaard — the city's position at the crossroads of all major transit routes makes it the de facto seat of road-law authority, and any amendment to that framework requires Midgaard's participation.

The Continuity faction within Midgaard's governance has opposed sky-road recognition on grounds that it articulates clearly and publicly: recognizing sky-roads as infrastructure creates precedent for non-human infrastructure claims generally. If aerial corridors have legal standing, the Deltari's claim that their hydraulic engineering constitutes infrastructure deserving governance recognition becomes harder to dismiss. If the Sky-Road Compact's territorial protocols have standing in corridor law, the Parallel Record Compact's documentation of institutional patterns becomes harder to classify as private community records without legal relevance. The Continuity faction's position is not that sky-roads don't exist or that the Serathi have no legitimate interest — it is that the specific legal form of recognition requested has systemic implications that extend well beyond the immediate question.

This analysis is correct. The Reckoning faction, which supports sky-road recognition, does not dispute it; they argue that the systemic implications are precisely the point. The Continuity faction has successfully blocked the recognition question from reaching corridor law amendment proceedings for two generations, primarily by ensuring the question is categorized as an internal Kiess matter rather than a corridor law matter requiring inter-city deliberation.

The Serathi sky-readers' response to this has been to document it. The roost-chronicles from the relevant periods include precise records of which Midgaard officials argued for which categorizations, in what proceedings, and with what effect. The chronicles note, without editorial comment, which of those officials had simultaneous commercial interests in the ridge-line construction projects that sky-road recognition would have required to be negotiated. The chronicles do not draw conclusions from this. They record what was observed.

### The Roost-Chronicle Accountability Record

The sky-readers' practice of documenting institutional processes from altitude creates an accountability record that no ground-based record-keeping system produces: a continuous, unbroken observation of what human governance does, recorded by people who are not party to the governance's internal deliberations and cannot be influenced by those deliberations' political pressures.

This record has become, in the context of the sky-road recognition dispute, something that Midgaard officials find uncomfortable. The sky-readers observed and documented not only the official proceedings but the informal pre-session conversations, the post-session caucuses visible from rooftop vantage, and the patterns of who met whom in what order in the days before and after significant votes. The sky-readers read the human city the way they read the sky: by watching the overall pattern of movement and inferring from it the forces at work.

Several Midgaard officials who have been involved in blocking the recognition question have quietly inquired, through intermediaries, whether the relevant roost-chronicle sections could be made unavailable to the Reckoning faction's researchers. The sky-readers have responded with consistent clarity: the chronicles are not available in physical form to any party for any purpose. What is offered is recitation, by sky-readers, of sections relevant to specific questions. The chronicles cannot be made unavailable because they have never been made available. They are simply there, maintained in high roosts that are Serathi territory, by Serathi custodians, for Serathi purposes.

This response has not satisfied the inquiring officials. It has also not produced any mechanism by which they can change the situation.

## The Mafdet Dimension

A recent development in the sky-road recognition dispute involves Mafdet, the eastern port city, whose commercial relationship with Kowloon has been expanding through the Saltglass Reach corridor. Mafdet's maritime legal tradition — the Littoral Branch Measure Script system — includes provisions for aerial transit rights that the inland corridor law does not, because maritime law has historically needed to address the intersection of sea transit, coastal air transit (primarily relevant for cargo birds and messenger raptors), and the sovereignty questions that arise when transit routes cross multiple jurisdictions.

The Serathi sky-readers near the eastern approaches — a smaller population than the western sky-roads communities but one with roost-chronicles covering the Saltglass Reach's aerial profile in considerable detail — have quietly engaged with Mafdet's Ledger Houses about whether Littoral Branch provisions for aerial transit rights could be extended to sky-road corridor recognition in a form that does not require Midgaard's participation.

The Ledger Houses are interested. The precedent would expand their legal system's scope in ways that benefit their commercial position relative to the inland corridor law system. The sky-readers are cautious: a recognition framework that applies only in Mafdet's jurisdiction solves the immediate encroachment problem in the Saltglass Reach but does not address the western corridor dispute and could create an inconsistent patchwork that weakens the argument for comprehensive corridor law reform.

The conversation is at an early stage. The sky-readers are not in a hurry. They are watching to see how the Kowloon-Mafdet commercial relationship develops and whether the Littoral Branch framework's expansion creates conditions that change Midgaard's calculation about the costs of continued non-engagement.

## Current State

The sky-road recognition dispute is in its third generation of active negotiation, which means that the Serathi sky-readers currently engaged in the process are not the sky-readers who began it. The roost-chronicles have been passed from one generation of sky-readers to the next. The documentation is continuous. The accountability record grows.

On the human institutional side, Midgaard has a new cohort of Continuity faction leadership whose institutional memory of the dispute's origins is secondhand. The Kiess Syndic Council has members who have grown up with the provisional Thornwall Corridor recognition as settled fact rather than temporary compromise, and who regard the failure to formalize it as an ongoing administrative irregularity. The Reckoning faction in Midgaard includes researchers who have engaged with the Serathi's roost-chronicle recitation process and who have begun to understand that the chronicles constitute a form of institutional accountability documentation that they have not had access to before.

The Serathi position has not changed. The sky-roads are infrastructure. Recognition is what infrastructure receives. The appropriate form of recognition is for the parties who govern the infrastructure below to acknowledge the infrastructure above. The sky-readers will continue to document what they observe, offer recitation of the chronicles to parties who engage in good faith with recognition questions, and maintain the high roosts from which they have observed the western territories since before Kiess existed and Evermeet was still a city.

The sky does not lie. The chronicles reflect what the sky shows. The sky-readers are prepared to wait as long as the sky.
