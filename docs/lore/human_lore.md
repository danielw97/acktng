# Lore: Humans

## Core Identity

Humans are the world's most widespread and institutionally dominant people — not because they are its strongest, most magical, or most perceptive inhabitants, but because they are its most relentless builders. They build roads, cities, legal systems, archives, and hierarchies with an urgency that other races find simultaneously admirable and alarming, as though they sense that any pause will cause everything to collapse back into chaos.

The world's major institutions — the Keeper civilization's road networks, the Solar Court's Great Pyramid, the Salt Ledger Kingdoms, the oasis corridor's water law, Midgaard's civic governance, Kowloon's delta trade empire — are human constructions. The world's most catastrophic institutional failures — the Spirebound Conclave's abandoned experiments, the sealed warrants and condemned-labor transfers, the Black Ledger, the Ashfall Era's compounded disasters — are also human constructions. Humans do not occupy a moral position in the world's lore so much as they occupy a **causal** position: most of what shapes this world, for good and ill, traces back to a human decision made inside a human institution.

This is not presented as praise or condemnation. It is the world's governing fact.

---

## The Defining Characteristic: Institutional Creatures

If a single characteristic separates humans from other peoples, it is their drive to create and inhabit **institutions**. Where a Khenari psychopomp records death as a sacred personal act, humans build courts and bureaucracies to handle death systematically. Where a Khephari elder reads the earth and warns of seismic patterns, humans build survey offices, file the reports, and sometimes act on them — and sometimes do not. Where Deltari water-readers track a river's behavior over generations, humans build water-law codes, assign enforcement authority, and debate the codes across political generations.

Institutions amplify human capability enormously. The Great Pyramid is not conceivable as a project of individual effort or even small-group effort; it required centuries of coordinated institutional action, hierarchical command, shared resource management, and preserved technical knowledge. The Keeper civilization's road network — arguably the most durable infrastructure in the world — was built by surveyors and engineers working within a coordinating institutional framework that maintained standards across vast distances and multiple generations.

But institutions also amplify human failures. The Spirebound Conclave did not accidentally harm people; it was institutionally organized to consume them, with documented supply chains, transfer protocols, and procurement agreements. The sealed warrants of Midgaard's Ash-and-Iron Decades were not individual crimes but institutionally processed documents flowing through normal administrative channels. The Black Ledger is not a personal atrocity but a bureaucratic artifact — a census. The thing that makes human institutions powerful is the same thing that makes human institutional crimes so thorough: **systematization**.

## History Through Institutional Eras

### The Primordial Age and the First Kings

Before institutions, there was the **Black Sun Shard** — a fragment of anti-light that consumed names, certainties, and memory. The first human kings who survived the Shard's fall learned a lesson that shaped all subsequent human civilization:

> To govern is to control what can be remembered.

The drive to record, catalog, and preserve that runs through every subsequent human institution begins here. The Shard taught humans that what is unrecorded is lost, and what is lost can unmake you.

### The Keeper Civilization: The Age of Measures

The Keepers of Measure are the earliest human civilization with comprehensive surviving records. They built the road networks, cistern systems, and calibrated administrative infrastructure that still underlie much of the world's physical landscape. Their governing philosophy was **measurement as governance**: if you can measure something precisely, you can administer it correctly; if you cannot measure it, you cannot administer it at all.

Keeper institutions were harsh by later standards — they treated mortality as a logistics variable, catalogued bodies on the road for administrative rather than compassionate purposes — but they were coherent. Their roads still function. Their cistern calculations still work. The Keepers' physical legacy outlasted every subsequent civilization that built on top of it.

Their cultural legacy is more complicated. The Keeper emphasis on measurement and record-keeping was inherited by every subsequent human institution, including those that used it to harm people. The bureaucratic machinery that made the Great Pyramid possible also made the Conclave's condemned-labor pipeline possible. The same technology.

### The Solar Court Era: The Great Pyramid

The Solar Court under Pharaoh Ramesses represents the apex of human cosmic-institutional ambition: the construction of a machine that could contain the Black Sun Shard, judge souls, and stabilize the world's memory against anti-light dissolution. The **Seal College** mathematicians who designed the Pyramid's binding systems were the most sophisticated institutional thinkers human civilization has produced — people who understood that true cosmic law required autonomous judgment, not mere mechanical execution, and who built the **Ushabti** accordingly.

The Solar Court's achievement is permanent. The Pyramid still functions. The Shard is still contained. Whatever else humans have done in the world, the Great Pyramid is an unambiguous good — a machine that preserves the possibility of memory and judgment against the force that would dissolve them.

The Solar Court itself did not survive. Its collapse is the clearest evidence that even the most successful human institutional project does not guarantee the survival of the institution that created it. The Ushabti continue operating according to the Solar Court's design — but without the Solar Court's oversight, without its hierarchy, without its ability to issue updated guidance. They serve a human vision of cosmic law that humans no longer formally maintain.

### The Salt Ledger Kingdoms: Merchant Governance

When the Solar Court fragmented, power consolidated in the hands of the Salt Ledger dynasties — merchant governance organized around logistics rather than cosmic authority. Where the Solar Court had built institutions to manage cosmic forces, the Salt Ledger Kingdoms built institutions to manage trade routes, debt, and resource distribution.

This era produced **Akh'enet** as a mortuary-port city — the point where caravan logistics merged with funerary administration in a way that made legal and death-processing functions indistinguishable. It also produced the framework that the Khenari absorbed and formalized: the idea that death is a transaction requiring administrative closure.

### The Twin Sovereigns: Institutional Schism

The political split between the Sun Sovereign (surface trade) and the Moon Sovereign (underground, death rites) formalized a tension that had existed since the Solar Court's founding: the question of whether cosmic law was primarily about the living world of commerce and governance or the spiritual world of death and judgment. Both Sovereigns claimed legitimacy from the Pyramid; neither fully represented it.

This schism permanently shaped the corridor's institutional culture. The Reed Ledger Wardens (Deltari-affiliated, practical, water-first) and the Jackal Balance Court (Khenari-affiliated, ritual, death-administrative) represent, in compressed form, the same structural disagreement: survival vs. ceremony, practical record vs. metaphysical sanction.

### The Ashfall Era: Catastrophe and Institutional Collapse

The volcanic sequence from the Cinderteeth Mountains — driven by the deep fault coupling that synchronized mountain seismic events with the oasis corridor — ended the institutional stability of the Salt Ledger period and produced the world's most concentrated human institutional failure: the rise of the **Spirebound Conclave** as an academy that used isolation and emergency governance to insulate itself from accountability.

The Conclave began as preservation scholarship: an institution organized around the three-element doctrine of flame, crystal, and spirit, attempting to bind and preserve dangerous forces. Over time, its research required human subjects. Its location in the Cinderteeth guaranteed the supply chain from condemnable populations in Midgaard and Kowloon. Its isolation from oversight guaranteed that no one checked whether its methods were acceptable.

The results:
- The **Withered Depths** — fungal blight created by abandoned crystal-matrix experiments that produced the Rivennid and continue to corrupt the northern forest
- The **Sunken Sanctum** — the Conclave's own collapse, its preserved records both a resource and an indictment
- The condemned-labor transfer records — the sealed warrants of Midgaard and the Black Ledger of Kowloon, which both cities still refuse to fully confront

The Ashfall Era taught — or should have taught — that institutional isolation and emergency governance together produce the conditions for atrocity. Every human institution that has existed since carries the shadow of that lesson.

### The Present: Midgaard, Kowloon, and the Reckoning

Contemporary human civilization is organized primarily around two cities:

**Midgaard** — the institutional crossroads city whose governance traces through the First Stone Accord, Lantern Reforms, Violet Compact, and Ash-and-Iron Decades. It is a city that has institutionalized guilt: the Continuity faction preserves stability by keeping the sealed warrants sealed; the Reckoning faction argues that no stable future is possible on an unacknowledged past. The city's arcanist registry contains "anomalous decay pattern" reports from forest scouts — evidence of the Withered Depths corruption — filed without action by both factions for different reasons.

**Kowloon** — the delta city built on Deltari foundations, governed by five houses whose authority traces through both legitimate commerce and the Black Ledger's sealed secrets. The Jade Magistracy's refusal to open the Black Ledger is simultaneously a legal decision, a political choice, and a daily demonstration that some human institutions will choose comfortable suppression over difficult truth indefinitely.

Both cities know what they did. Both have chosen, in different ways, not to fully say so.

## Human Diversity

Humans are not a monolith. The world's human population encompasses:

- **Midgaard's civic bureaucrats**, organized around institutional continuity and guild function
- **Kowloon's delta merchants**, built on Deltari water-knowledge and Salt Ledger trade law
- **Desert caravan operators**, maintaining the road network under conditions that blend Keeper logistics with Salt Ledger commercial law
- **Ash Cult adherents**, who read the Cinderteeth as a judicial instrument and whose theology preserves the oldest surviving echo of Ventspeaker practice
- **Oasis administrators**, managing the water-rights infrastructure that keeps the corridor alive
- **Pyramid approach community members**, living in the zone where Solar Court theology and practical desert survival intersect daily
- **Mountain-adjacent communities** who learned, the hard way, what the Cinderteeth can do

This diversity means that no single description of "human culture" is accurate. The human who serves as a notary in Midgaard's legal district and the human who interprets volcanic vents as divine pronouncements in an Ash Cult cell are operating from radically different institutional frameworks, even though they share the same racial classification.

## Racial Traits

**Stat Profile:** STR 19 | INT 19 | WIS 19 | DEX 19 | CON 19

Human stats are perfectly balanced — not high in any category, not low in any. This is not a limitation but a design statement: humans are the race for whom adaptation is the primary survival strategy. They will never be the strongest, smartest, fastest, or toughest being in a given encounter. They will, in the right circumstances, be the most capable of doing whatever that encounter requires.

**Elemental Affinities:** None.

Humans have no elemental affinities, no elemental weaknesses, no elemental resistances, and no elemental susceptibilities. This is the most important fact about human magic. They do not resonate with any particular force in the world; they work with all of them with equal (and equally unpracticed) facility.

This creates a specific kind of human mage: not a fire specialist who calls flame instinctively, but a student of fire who learned the calling and can also, with effort, learn water. Not a divine channeler whose power flows from cosmic alignment, but a practitioner who developed technique. Human mastery of magic is always earned, never inherited — which means human magical tradition produces the broadest possible range of magical capability at the cost of producing no one for whom magic is natural.

**Racial Skills:** None.

Humans have no inherent racial skills. They bring no natural advantages to combat, stealth, scholarship, or spiritual practice. What they bring is the capacity to develop those advantages through practice, instruction, and institutional transmission — the accumulated knowledge of predecessors organized into teachable form.

In a world where most races have at least one inherent advantage that defines their relationship to it, humans are defined by the absence of such definition. They are whatever they make themselves. They are whatever their institutions make them.

This is, depending on who you ask, either humans' greatest strength or their most dangerous characteristic.

## Relationship to the Other Peoples

### Khenari

The relationship between humans and the Khenari is organized around mutual utility and mutual suspicion. Humans rely on Khenari notaries, estate administrators, and death-record keepers because Khenari accuracy is unmatched and their incorruptibility is genuine. Khenari rely on human institutions for the civic frameworks within which their death-administration practice operates.

The suspicion runs in both directions. Human institutions distrust the Khenari's incorruptibility precisely because they need to be able to pressure notaries occasionally, and incorruptible notaries are unmanageable. The Khenari distrust human institutions because they can see, in every record they process, how often those institutions are built on false premises, falsified documents, and suppressed evidence.

The Khenari's relationship to Akh'enet's ruins — their pilgrimage practice, their factionalism, their theological debates — mostly happens without human participation. This is by design. The three-way conflict between Reed Ledger Wardens, Moon Sepulchrists, and Salt Jackal Reclaimers is Khenari internal politics, and Khenari are deeply reluctant to allow human institutional interest to define its outcome. Too many times in their history, the relevant human institution decided to simplify that conflict by elevating whichever faction was most convenient.

See also: `khenari_lore.md`

### Khephari

The Khephari built the roads to and from the Great Pyramid. They know this, and they remember it quietly when human institutions describe the Pyramid as a Solar Court achievement. The Khephari contribution is there in the stone — in the road gradients, the distance-markers, the tunnel alignments — and the Khephari do not need it acknowledged to know it is true. But they notice when it is not acknowledged, and they file that observation in the same patient accounting they apply to everything.

The relationship is not hostile. Humans and Khephari share the desert road network in genuinely collaborative ways: Khephari earth-sense and Khephari local knowledge are essential to safe caravan operation, and human commercial infrastructure provides the trading ecosystem within which Khephari service communities operate. The cooperation is real.

What is also real: the Khephari's seismic warnings about the Cinderteeth were documented, submitted to Keeper survey offices, and filed without action. The Ashfall catastrophe that followed killed tens of thousands of people. The Khephari do not think much about blame — the cycle continues regardless — but they think carefully about which human institutions actually hear what is put before them, and which only receive it.

See also: `khephari_lore.md`

### Ashborn

Humans made the Ashborn and then disputed whether they had. The Ashfall Era's volcanic events transformed a subset of human survivors into something physiologically distinct — and then human institutions, specifically Midgaard's post-Ashfall governance, classified the Ashborn as "ashfall survivors" without separate civic identity in order to deny them legal standing in property and inheritance disputes during reconstruction.

This is one of the world's cleaner examples of institutional bad faith. The Ashborn existed; their existence was inconvenient for property redistribution; the relevant institution redefined them out of civic standing. The Reckoning faction in Midgaard argues this explicitly; the Continuity faction's silence on the subject is its own answer.

The Ashborn themselves are not animated by grievance. Their culture processes damage without resentment — the cycle continues, the repair is slow, the work goes on. They remain in Midgaard's foundry districts, remain essential to the city's metalwork economy, and remain classified in some official records as a subcategory of human rather than a distinct people. The classification does not change what they are.

See also: `ashborn_lore.md`

### Umbral

The Umbral are, in a sense, the human past made visible. Their shadow-archives contain records that Midgaard and Kowloon would prefer not to have accessible — the actual history of decisions made, people transferred, warrants issued, and accountability avoided. They exist in the underground spaces of human cities, maintaining parallel records that undermine official histories, and human institutions cannot decide whether to regard them as a resource or a threat.

Both, mostly. The Reckoning faction cultivates Umbral archive access because those archives contain the sealed warrant evidence that Continuity suppresses. The Continuity faction tolerates Umbral communities because the Umbral are genuinely useful in legal and intelligence functions that require absolute discretion. Neither faction can fully trust the Umbral because the Umbral's primary loyalty is to the accuracy of their records, not to any human political outcome.

The Gloamvault — the oldest continuous shadow-archive in the known world — is Umbral territory that exists under Midgaard. The city was built above the Vault; the Umbral were there first. This fact sits in Midgaard's civic consciousness as one of many things it would prefer not to think about carefully.

See also: `umbral_lore.md`

### Rivennid

The Rivennid are the Conclave's legacy, which means they are, indirectly, Midgaard's and Kowloon's legacy as well. The institutional chain is direct and documented: both cities' condemned-labor transfer programs supplied the Conclave's research subjects; the Conclave's Prism Thesis field trials implanted crystal matrices in the forest; the abandoned matrices produced the Withered Depths' corruption and, eventually, the Rivennid themselves.

The Rivennid do not call this a debt. They call it a **fact**. They are careful about this distinction: they are not pursuing compensation or revenge, and they are not organized around grievance. They are organized around the principle that institutions which suppress their worst decisions tend to repeat them. Both Midgaard and Kowloon have suppressed the records that connect them to the Conclave transfers. Both cities' northern trade routes are affected by the corruption those transfers ultimately produced. The Rivennid find this instructive.

Human relationship with the Rivennid is largely confined to the communities near Kiess who trade with Rivennid travelers for medicinal fungi and forest intelligence. Most humans in Midgaard or Kowloon have never met a Rivennid. The connection between their cities' institutional history and the Rivennid's existence is not part of any civic curriculum.

See also: `rivennid_lore.md`

### Deltari

The Deltari built Kowloon. This is not metaphor. The delta's hydraulic infrastructure — the channel management, water-quality monitoring, flood-defense systems, and cistern networks that keep the city alive — was built by Deltari communities who had been managing that delta for generations before the city's founding institutions arrived. Kowloon is built on Deltari foundations, physically and technically.

The Jade Magistracy's governance of that city excludes Deltari communities from formal power while depending entirely on their practical expertise. The Black Ledger — the sealed census of individuals transferred to the Conclave — lists Deltari among those transferred, names that Deltari oral tradition has preserved across generations while the official records remained sealed.

Human relationship with the Deltari is the most nakedly extractive of any inter-racial dynamic in the world. The Deltari contribute the expertise that makes the city function; the human institutions take the civic authority and the trade revenue. Deltari communities maintain parallel records that document this arrangement in detail, which is the most effective leverage they have and the primary reason the Jade Magistracy has not found a way to fully suppress them.

See also: `deltari_lore.md`

### Ushabti

Humans made the Ushabti to embody justice, then failed to maintain the civilization that authorized them. The Solar Court's Seal College constructed the Ushabti as autonomous vessels of cosmic law; the Solar Court subsequently collapsed, leaving the Ushabti operating without institutional oversight, without a hierarchy to report to, without the ability to have their variance records formally reviewed.

The Ushabti continue because they decided that the Solar Court's purpose — containing the Black Sun Shard, maintaining cosmic law, preserving the conditions for memory and judgment — did not expire with the Solar Court's institutions. This is the most charitable reading of autonomous action without authorization that exists in the world, and most humans who encounter Ushabti find it persuasive because the Ushabti's judgment is demonstrably good.

What humans find uncomfortable is the implication: if the Solar Court's collapse did not end the legitimacy of its purpose, what does that say about other human institutions that have collapsed? If the Ushabti are right to continue serving justice without institutional sanction, are there other obligations — to the Ashborn misclassified after the Ashfall, to the families in the Black Ledger, to the forest poisoned by Conclave experiments — that also survive the collapse of the institutions that created them?

The Ushabti do not push this argument explicitly. They do not need to. They exist, and their existence asks the question.

See also: `ushabti_lore.md`

## The Sealed Secrets

Any comprehensive account of humans in this world must acknowledge the sealed records that sit at the center of their institutional history.

**The Sealed Warrants** — Midgaard's Ash-and-Iron Decades produced documented warrant records condemning debtors, petty criminals, and "unregistered persons" to labor service. The Violet Compact's registry processed these individuals as "arcane material," removing their civic identity. The Spirebound Conclave received them for experimentation. The warrants are sealed in Midgaard's archives. The Continuity faction considers this a matter of stability; the Reckoning faction considers it a matter of justice.

**The Black Ledger** — Kowloon's sealed census of individuals transferred to the Conclave from the delta settlements. Proto-Kowloon's founding institutions participated in the same condemned-labor transfer that Midgaard's institutions participated in. The Black Ledger contains the unredacted names. It is sealed in an undercity cistern. The Jade Magistracy declines to discuss it.

**The Conclave Records** — The Sunken Sanctum's surviving documentation of what was done with the transferred individuals, the crystal-binding experiments, the forest trials, and the failure modes. The Conclave collapsed, but its records survived. The Rivennid have particular interest in these records; so do the scholars trying to understand the Withered Depths' corruption.

These records are not secrets that humans lack the ability to open. They are secrets that the relevant human institutions have chosen to keep sealed, because the process of opening them would require acknowledging institutional crimes that the current civic order partially descends from.

Other peoples — Khenari notaries, Umbral shadow-archivists, Deltari flow-record keepers, Rivennid network-memories — hold parallel records that corroborate or surpass what the sealed human archives contain. The human institutions that maintain these sealed records are the only parties who do not already know what is in them.

## Narrative Tone

Human NPCs should feel like people operating inside institutions that are larger than them, shaped by histories they know imperfectly, making decisions inside frameworks they did not create and cannot unilaterally change. This is not an excuse; it is a texture. The Midgaard clerk who processes a warrant is not the same moral actor as the magistrate who designed the warrant system, but neither is entirely innocent.

The most interesting human characters in this world are those who have some awareness of the institutional chains they are embedded in — who know that their city's foundations were built by Deltari, who have heard the rumors about the sealed warrants, who have noticed that the northern timber is a little shorter this season — and who have made some kind of decision about what to do with that awareness. Not all of them make admirable decisions. But awareness creates the possibility of accountability, and accountability is the one thing that distinguishes human institutional history from being purely a record of managed decline.

Humans built the world. They are still deciding what to do about it.
