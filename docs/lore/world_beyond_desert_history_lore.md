# Lore: World History Beyond the Eastern Desert

This file documents three under-recorded dimensions of world history:

1. **The Salt Ledger Kingdoms' external trading partners** (Era IV) — the peoples and communities the desert civilization traded with beyond its own road network.
2. **The Ninth Meridian Collapse's global effects** (Era VII) — what happened to the rest of the world when the eastern trade network shattered.
3. **The Week of Impossible Dawns' global reach** (Era VIII) — how the Black Sun Shard's near-containment-failure registered across regions with no obelisk network.

The eastern desert's own lore is documented in `eastern_desert_lore.md`, `great_pyramid_lore.md`, and the relevant area lore files. This file exists specifically to fill the space outside that core, where those events were felt secondhand, misunderstood, or never fully recorded at all.

## Part I: The Salt Ledger Kingdoms and Their External Trading Partners (Era IV)

The Salt Ledger Kingdoms were the wealthiest organized civilization of their era, but they were not the world. The road network they maintained was long, but it had edges, and beyond those edges were peoples with goods, knowledge, and leverage the desert civilization needed.

The Kings themselves understood this. The crystalline salt tablets that gave the era its name were not merely internal accounting media — they circulated outward, carried by caravans that returned with commodities no desert environment could produce. The Shoreward Assessors who formalized the Saltglass Reach's maritime conversion law did so because maritime goods were already arriving; the legal infrastructure followed the trade, not the other way around.

What follows are the major external trading relationships that sustained the Salt Ledger economy. None are documented in a single source. They are reconstructed from Keeper survey records, Kethari water-memory archives, Serathi roost-chronicles, and scattered Deltari reed-ledger fragments — all of which agree on the broad shape while disagreeing on the details.

---

### The Kethari (Saltglass Reach)

The Kethari were the Salt Ledger Kingdoms' most important non-desert trading partner, and the relationship predated the Kingdoms entirely. The Aquifer Covenant governing Deepwell Confluence water claims was Kethari law before any human institution existed to recognize or contest it; the Salt Ledger Kings inherited the covenant as a constraint and spent generations negotiating around it.

What the Kethari traded: subsurface water access beyond what the Deepwell Confluence's public springs provided, knowledge of secondary aquifer fractures that surface surveys had failed to locate, coastal goods from Saltglass tidemouth communities, and crystalline salt compounds extracted from subsurface mineral deposits — salt of higher purity than anything the surface mines produced, used in both preservation and high-precision ritual contexts.

What the Salt Ledger Kings offered in return: surface goods (craft items, textiles, processed foods), formal legal recognition of Kethari territorial claims along the Reach, and protection from charter road militias who might otherwise have treated Kethari subsurface corridors as unowned territory. The Shoreward Assessors who emerged as the Reach's first permanent professional class built their translation protocols partly to handle Kethari goods, which moved under different legal frameworks than caravan goods and required specialist clerks who understood both systems.

The relationship was never equal. The Kethari held the aquifer; the Kings held the roads. Neither could afford to break the other. This mutual dependency produced a negotiating tradition that the Kethari maintained long after the Salt Ledger Kingdoms collapsed — the habit of dealing with surface powers from a position of knowledge-leverage rather than territorial force. It is why the Kethari survived the Charter Wars intact when surface-dependent peoples did not.

### The Serathi (Western Sky-Roads)

The Serathi provided something the road network could not: reach. Their sky-road corridors above the territory that would become Roc Road connected the Salt Ledger Kingdoms to regions whose ground routes were too distant, too seasonal, or too dangerous for regular caravan traffic. The Salt Ledger Kings hired Serathi sky-readers as route scouts, storm-warning specialists, and long-distance diplomatic couriers.

What the Serathi traded: aerial reconnaissance of route conditions ahead of major caravans, storm-warning data that allowed cistern-relay timing adjustments, exotic goods from distant regions inaccessible by land, and cross-regional intelligence — information about political conditions, raider activity, and resource availability in places no caravan master had visited. This last commodity was priced separately from physical goods and is the oldest documented example of information sold as a commodity rather than exchanged as a courtesy.

What the Kings offered in return: food stores maintained at specific roost-points along trade routes (a logistical concession that cost the Kingdoms significantly, since roost-point provisioning required maintaining supply chains to locations with no commercial traffic), formal legal protection of Serathi sky-road corridors from claim by surface-bound route operators, and commodity goods — metals, textiles — not available in highland environments.

This arrangement gave the Serathi their first sustained contact with the Keeper-of-Measure record tradition. Serathi couriers who served as diplomatic intermediaries were exposed to the Measure Script system and its combinatorial precision. The roost-chronicle methodology that the Serathi developed — methodical, timestamped, calibrated against multiple external references — reflects the Keeper influence without replicating it. The Serathi adapted precision documentation to aerial observation: where Keeper survey records encoded direction, time, volume, and legal status in each glyph, Serathi roost-chronicles encode position, altitude, atmospheric condition, and observed-event reliability. The underlying philosophy of treating observation as legislation is the same.
