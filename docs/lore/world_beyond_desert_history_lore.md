# Lore: World History Beyond the Eastern Desert

This file documents three under-recorded dimensions of world history:

1. **The Salt Ledger Kingdoms' external trading partners** (Era IV) — the peoples and communities the desert civilization traded with beyond its own road network.
2. **The Ninth Meridian Collapse's global effects** (Era VII) — what happened to the rest of the world when the eastern trade network shattered.
3. **The Week of Impossible Dawns' global reach** (Era VIII) — how the Black Sun Shard's near-containment-failure registered across regions with no obelisk network.

The eastern desert's own lore is documented in `eastern_desert_lore.md`, `great_pyramid_lore.md`, and the relevant area lore files. This file exists specifically to fill the space outside that core, where those events were felt secondhand, misunderstood, or never fully recorded at all.

## Part I: The Salt Ledger Kingdoms and Their External Trading Partners (Era IV)

The Salt Ledger Kingdoms were the wealthiest organized civilization of their era, but they were not the world. The road network they maintained was long, but it had edges, and beyond those edges were peoples with goods, knowledge, and leverage the desert civilization needed.

The Kings themselves understood this. The crystalline salt tablets that gave the era its name were not merely internal accounting media — they circulated outward, carried by caravans that returned with commodities no desert environment could produce. The Shoreward Assessors who formalized the Saltglass Reach's maritime conversion law did so because maritime goods were already arriving; the legal infrastructure followed the trade, not the other way around.

What follows are the major external trading relationships that sustained the Salt Ledger economy. None are documented in a single source. They are reconstructed from Keeper survey records, Kethari water-memory archives, Serathi roost-chronicles, and scattered Deltari reed-ledger fragments — all of which agree on the broad shape while disagreeing on the details.

---

### The Kethari (Saltglass Reach)

The Kethari were the Salt Ledger Kingdoms' most important non-desert trading partner, and the relationship predated the Kingdoms entirely. The Aquifer Covenant governing Deepwell Confluence water claims was Kethari law before any human institution existed to recognize or contest it; the Salt Ledger Kings inherited the covenant as a constraint and spent generations negotiating around it.

What the Kethari traded: subsurface water access beyond what the Deepwell Confluence's public springs provided, knowledge of secondary aquifer fractures that surface surveys had failed to locate, coastal goods from Saltglass tidemouth communities, and crystalline salt compounds extracted from subsurface mineral deposits — salt of higher purity than anything the surface mines produced, used in both preservation and high-precision ritual contexts.

What the Salt Ledger Kings offered in return: surface goods (craft items, textiles, processed foods), formal legal recognition of Kethari territorial claims along the Reach, and protection from charter road militias who might otherwise have treated Kethari subsurface corridors as unowned territory. The Shoreward Assessors who emerged as the Reach's first permanent professional class built their translation protocols partly to handle Kethari goods, which moved under different legal frameworks than caravan goods and required specialist clerks who understood both systems.

The relationship was never equal. The Kethari held the aquifer; the Kings held the roads. Neither could afford to break the other. This mutual dependency produced a negotiating tradition that the Kethari maintained long after the Salt Ledger Kingdoms collapsed — the habit of dealing with surface powers from a position of knowledge-leverage rather than territorial force. It is why the Kethari survived the Charter Wars intact when surface-dependent peoples did not.

### The Serathi (Western Sky-Roads)

The Serathi provided something the road network could not: reach. Their sky-road corridors above the territory that would become Roc Road connected the Salt Ledger Kingdoms to regions whose ground routes were too distant, too seasonal, or too dangerous for regular caravan traffic. The Salt Ledger Kings hired Serathi sky-readers as route scouts, storm-warning specialists, and long-distance diplomatic couriers.

What the Serathi traded: aerial reconnaissance of route conditions ahead of major caravans, storm-warning data that allowed cistern-relay timing adjustments, exotic goods from distant regions inaccessible by land, and cross-regional intelligence — information about political conditions, raider activity, and resource availability in places no caravan master had visited. This last commodity was priced separately from physical goods and is the oldest documented example of information sold as a commodity rather than exchanged as a courtesy.

What the Kings offered in return: food stores maintained at specific roost-points along trade routes (a logistical concession that cost the Kingdoms significantly, since roost-point provisioning required maintaining supply chains to locations with no commercial traffic), formal legal protection of Serathi sky-road corridors from claim by surface-bound route operators, and commodity goods — metals, textiles — not available in highland environments.

This arrangement gave the Serathi their first sustained contact with the Keeper-of-Measure record tradition. Serathi couriers who served as diplomatic intermediaries were exposed to the Measure Script system and its combinatorial precision. The roost-chronicle methodology that the Serathi developed — methodical, timestamped, calibrated against multiple external references — reflects the Keeper influence without replicating it. The Serathi adapted precision documentation to aerial observation: where Keeper survey records encoded direction, time, volume, and legal status in each glyph, Serathi roost-chronicles encode position, altitude, atmospheric condition, and observed-event reliability. The underlying philosophy of treating observation as legislation is the same.

### The Khephari (Ochre Basin Communities)

The Khephari were not a unified people in the way the Kethari or Serathi were. The name is a Salt Ledger administrative designation — a single entry in the Shoreward Assessors' classification system for a loose cluster of communities in the Ochre Basin who shared material culture and trade habits without sharing governance. The Khephari themselves used no collective name; individual communities identified by their nearest water feature or ancestral lineage marker.

What the Khephari traded: processed ochre pigments used in ritual contexts across the Salt Ledger Kingdoms, ceramic goods fired in techniques not available in the desert (the particular silicate composition of Ochre Basin clay produced a fired surface the Kings' court workshops could not replicate), sun-dried fish from seasonal lake pools that formed during wet cycles, and medicinal plants with documented therapeutic properties that the desert could not cultivate. The ochre was the most commercially significant. Salt Ledger ritual architecture required large quantities of prepared pigment, and the Khephari's processing methods — which involved staged mineral extraction rather than simple grinding — produced a purer colorant that required less binder to adhere properly.

What the Kings offered in return: salt (the Khephari had no good local salt source and relied on trade for preservation), metal tools, and road access for Khephari traders traveling to markets. The road access was the most meaningful concession. Ochre Basin communities were geographically positioned such that reaching any significant market required crossing charter road territory. Road permits were controlled by the Kings' infrastructure administration, and access could be granted, restricted, or revoked. The Khephari traded partly under the shadow of that leverage, which the Assessors were well aware of and the Kings periodically exploited.

The Khephari communities fragmented further during the Charter Wars, when their road access was disrupted and the market for their goods contracted sharply. Some communities survived by redirecting trade through Kethari subsurface corridors. Others did not survive as distinct communities at all, absorbed into larger regional populations. The Shoreward Assessors' classification system outlasted the people it classified — the administrative category "Khephari" persisted in bureaucratic records for two generations after the communities it described had ceased to exist in recognizable form.

### The Deltari (River Delta Communities)

The Deltari occupied the inland delta system west of the Saltglass Reach, where several seasonal rivers converged and spread before losing themselves in the sand. Unlike the desert interior, the delta could support intensive agriculture during good water years, and the Deltari had developed agricultural surplus management systems sophisticated enough to carry communities through bad years without catastrophic loss.

What the Deltari traded: grain (the most significant desert import — the Salt Ledger road network's provisioning system depended on stable grain supplies from outside the desert), reed goods (paper, basket goods, and structural materials), agricultural expertise that the Kingdoms hired as consultants for cistern-adjacent cultivation experiments, and fresh water in quantities the desert interior's aquifer access could not reliably provide. The grain relationship was existential on both sides: the Deltari needed the distribution market the road network provided (without it, surplus grain rotted faster than it could be consumed locally), and the Kingdoms needed the grain to sustain the large non-farming populations that road infrastructure and administration required.

What the Kings offered: salt (the delta salt supply was inconsistent; preservation reliability required desert-grade salt at scale), metal goods, manufactured textiles, and formal price guarantees. The price guarantees were the Shoreward Assessors' most ambitious external policy instrument: the Kings agreed to purchase minimum quantities of grain at guaranteed minimum prices regardless of market conditions, in exchange for supply commitments that prioritized the road network during shortage years. This system kept both parties stable but created a political dependency that became dangerous later. When the Ninth Meridian Collapse disrupted road network revenue, the Kings were still legally obligated to honor grain purchase contracts they could no longer afford.

The Deltari reed-ledger fragments that survive are the best external source on Salt Ledger commodity pricing. The Deltari kept meticulous transaction records — less elaborate than Keeper survey work, but consistent and durable — and those records preserve price data that the Kings' own archives did not maintain in accessible form. Most of what researchers know about Salt Ledger Era IV inflation rates comes from Deltari grain contract records, not from the desert's own administrative files.

### Western Woodland Communities

The western woodland peoples — recorded collectively in Assessor documents as the "Arboreal Covenant signatories," a grouping the communities themselves did not use — occupied forested highland territory that the desert road network did not reach. They were the Salt Ledger Kingdoms' most distant regular trading partners and the ones the Kingdoms understood least.

What the woodland communities traded: hardwood timber (essential for construction in a region with no native tree cover, and for the massive wooden mechanisms in the deeper Pyramid levels), resins and adhesives, preserved game, dyed textiles in color traditions unavailable in the desert, and — most unusually — live animals maintained for breeding stock, particularly drought-resistant cattle varieties adapted to highland conditions that performed better in the desert margins than lowland breeds.

What the Kings offered: salt, metal tools, and manufactured goods. The exchange was less institutionalized than the Kethari or Deltari relationships. Arboreal Covenant trade moved through intermediaries rather than direct diplomatic contact. Serathi sky-road couriers carried initial contact messages; Deltari traders who had independent relationships with both parties often served as brokers. The Kings' formal administrative apparatus never fully penetrated the woodland relationship. There are no Shoreward Assessors' files for Arboreal Covenant goods — they entered the desert supply chain already classified as third-party goods under Deltari or Serathi import categories.

This arm's-length relationship meant the woodland communities were largely unaffected by the Salt Ledger Kingdoms' political crises. When the Charter Wars disrupted the road network, they redirected their trade to other intermediaries without great difficulty. The category "Arboreal Covenant signatories" disappeared from the administrative record not because the communities dispersed but because the administrative apparatus that had been classifying them ceased to function.
