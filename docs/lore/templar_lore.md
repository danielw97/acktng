# Lore: The Templar Tradition

## Overview

The Templar title descends from the Solar Court's **temple-guard auxiliaries** — wardens who stood at the boundary between battle-clergy and ceremonial ministry, responsible for neither pure combat nor pure healing but both at once. After the Sundering Wars, the Paladin vanguard and the Priest ward-keepers settled into deliberately separate roles: one advancing, one sustaining. The synthesis was forced by necessity during the garrison crisis of the **Violet Compact's founding years**, when a combined Paladin-Priest force found itself too few to maintain separate functions and began training across doctrinal lines. They revived the old Solar Court title for what it meant: standing between two callings at once. The Violet Compact eventually absorbed the resulting Corps as a sanctioned order, codifying what had begun as improvised wartime necessity into a formal discipline — and into the adept path for those who had mastered both the oath-vanguard and the restorative minister.

## I. The Solar Court Temple-Guard Auxiliaries

The Solar Court's **temple-guard auxiliaries** are the earliest documented precedent for the Templar's dual-function practice — and are claimed as foundational ancestors by both the Paladin tradition and the Templar tradition, though through different evolutionary paths. Understanding the auxiliaries requires understanding the Solar Court's institutional structure.

The Solar Court maintained five offices: Dawn Liturgists, Ledger Priests, Embalmer Houses, Jackal Tribunal, and Seal College. Each office had a dedicated function and dedicated practitioners. But between the offices and the physical infrastructure they maintained (the Great Pyramid's shrines, processional routes, oath-halls, and administrative galleries), there was a layer of physical security and ceremonial maintenance that none of the five offices considered their primary responsibility. This function fell to the auxiliaries: wardens who managed the threshold between the sacred spaces and the broader world.

The auxiliaries' **dual-function requirement** was structural: they could not perform their threshold role by specializing in either combat or healing alone. A threshold warden who could defend against physical threats but could not consecrate a space after a desecration event was incomplete. A threshold warden who could perform consecration rites but could not prevent or respond to the desecrations that required consecration was also incomplete. The job required both capacities, not at the level of deep specialization the five offices achieved in their respective domains, but at the level of reliable operational competence across both.

The Paladin tradition claims the auxiliaries as proof that its oath-bound vanguard identity has Solar Court precedent — the auxiliaries were oath-bound to the sacred spaces they protected, not to specific commanders. The Templar tradition claims the auxiliaries as proof that its dual-function methodology has Solar Court precedent — the auxiliaries were explicitly required to perform both combat and ceremonial functions rather than specializing in one.

Both claims are historically accurate, which is why both traditions can point to the same origin without either tradition's claim being false. The difference is in emphasis: the Paladin tradition extracts the oath-binding as the foundational element; the Templar tradition extracts the dual-function requirement as the foundational element.

## II. The Sundering Wars and the Paladin-Priest Separation

The **Sundering Wars** produced the conditions for what became the Paladin-Priest separation: the high clerics who resolved never to leave sanctuaries defenseless again created the Paladin oath-bound vanguard, while the other faction — those who held that the tradition's foundational purpose was the service of living suffering — withdrew to concentrate on restoration and healing.

The separation was not, initially, intended as permanent. Both groups understood themselves as temporarily specializing within a single tradition to address the specific crisis conditions of the Sundering Wars. The Paladins concentrated on advance-and-sanctify because that was what the crisis immediately required. The emerging Priest lineage concentrated on restoration and ward-network development because the war's mass casualties and sanctuary desecrations required sustained healing capacity that divided attention couldn't provide.

The specializations that were temporary crisis responses became, over generations, permanent institutional identities. The Paladin academies developed training curricula optimized for the advance-and-sanctify function; the Priest communities developed training environments optimized for sustained restoration. Practitioners in each tradition developed skills that were genuinely specialized and specifically excellent in their domain, and found that the skills of the other domain had been absent from their training long enough that crossing the divide required something close to starting over.

The **Hour of Quiet Thunder** schism that formally established the Priest tradition as a distinct remort path was the institutional acknowledgment that this specialization was now permanent — not because the traditions held irreconcilable theological positions (they did not; both remained within the cleric tradition's broader framework) but because the specific practices had diverged enough that practitioners needed different formation processes.

## III. The Garrison Crisis

The **garrison crisis of the Violet Compact's founding years** is the practical event that produced the Templar synthesis. The Violet Compact's establishment required securing specific geographic points — waystation sites, border crossings, former Conclave-adjacent territories — that were simultaneously threatened by residual forces from the Ash-and-Iron Decades conflicts and in need of consecration services because previous occupants had left them spiritually compromised.

The Paladin-Priest forces assigned to garrison duty at these points found themselves in a specific operational bind: they were too few to maintain the separate Paladin advance-post and Priest interior functions that normal operational doctrine prescribed. The Paladin advance-post needed to remain mobile to respond to threats; the Priest interior needed to remain stable to maintain healing and consecration operations. With insufficient numbers to staff both functions simultaneously, something had to give.

The practitioners who worked through this problem were motivated by practical necessity rather than theoretical commitment. Some began learning the other tradition's techniques — Paladins taking minimal healer instruction, Priests taking minimal combat instruction — to cover functional gaps when numbers required it. Others began developing integrated techniques that served both functions simultaneously: striking techniques that incorporated consecration effects, healing techniques that could be performed with minimal positional stability requirements.

These practitioners found, unexpectedly, that integration at depth produced qualitatively different results than integration at the minimum-coverage level. A Paladin who had developed genuine healing competence (not just emergency first aid but the Priest tradition's sustained channel methodology) could maintain offensive pressure while simultaneously supporting allies in ways that neither the Paladin's advance doctrine nor the Priest's stability doctrine separately accommodated. A Priest who had developed genuine combat competence (not just minimal self-defense but the Paladin's advance-and-sanctify methodology) could conduct healing operations in active threat environments that would otherwise require a Paladin escort to secure.

The revived title of **Templar** — drawn from the Solar Court records that the garrison crisis practitioners were consulting for historical precedent on dual-function roles — became the designation for practitioners who had achieved genuine depth in both functions, not just minimum coverage of both.

## IV. The Enhanced Heal Integration

The Templar tradition's most technically sophisticated contribution is the **Enhanced Heal** technique — an integration of healing methodology with the status-cure functions (cure blindness, remove curse, cure poison) that had previously required separate sequential applications.

The Priest tradition's healing methodology was developed for specific damage types — physical wounds, structural injury, life-force depletion. Status conditions (blindness, curse, poison) were treated as distinct categories requiring different techniques. The sequence required to address a target who was both physically injured and poisoned was: heal the physical damage, then apply cure poison. This sequential approach created a time gap during which the target remained vulnerable to the active poison's continued effects.

The Enhanced Heal integration addresses this by applying the status-cure functions as part of the healing channel rather than as separate subsequent applications. The technique requires simultaneously maintaining the healing channel's primary physical restoration function while applying the three status-cure functions as subsidiary channels within the same working. This is substantially more technically demanding than sequential application but eliminates the treatment gap and can, in specific situations (targets with multiple simultaneous status conditions and physical damage), significantly improve outcomes compared to the sequential approach.

The technique's development during the garrison crisis period reflects the cross-training context that produced the Templar tradition: practitioners who were learning both Paladin offensive techniques and Priest healing techniques found that combining multiple functions within single working instances was a general skill that applied across domains, not a specialist technique limited to the healing context.

## V. Templars Within the Five Cities

The **Violet Compact's sanctioned Templar Corps** is the tradition's formal civic institutional expression — the garrison-crisis force that the Compact recognized as a formal order. The Corps operates across all five cities, maintaining the specific kind of dual-function force that the garrison crisis demonstrated was institutionally necessary in contexts requiring simultaneous combat capacity and sustained consecration/healing.

The Corps' sanctioned status creates an interesting political dynamic: Templars are simultaneously affiliated with civic authority (through the Compact's sanction) and with religious authority (through the Paladin and Priest traditions' theological frameworks). In most situations these authorities are aligned. When they diverge — when civic authority and religious commitment require different actions — the Templar tradition's Solar Court ancestry (auxiliary oath-bound to sacred spaces rather than to commanders) provides the doctrinal framework for how the conflict is resolved: the oath governs.
