# Lore: The Desert Compact

## A Precedence That Predates the Pyramid

The eastern peoples — Kethari, Khenari, Khephari, and Ushabti — share the desert and its margins in a relationship that human observers consistently misread as simple cohabitation. It is not. It is a structured order of precedence, informally maintained and never formally ratified, that determines whose knowledge takes priority on questions of deep time, whose institutional authority is recognized in which domains, and whose presence in a given territory carries what weight of implied claim.

This structure is called, among those who call it anything, the **Desert Compact** — a name that implies more formality than exists, since the Compact is simply the accumulated pattern of four peoples' mutual recognition over timescales that make formal documentation seem almost beside the point. The Pyramid is three eras old. The Compact is older than the first Pyramid survey.

## Order of Precedence

### First Presence: The Kethari

On any question of deep-time eastern desert knowledge, the Kethari have acknowledged precedence. This is not a courtesy granted by the other peoples; it is a recognition earned through the specificity and longevity of Kethari water-memory.

The Kethari were in the eastern desert before any other people whose oral tradition has been cross-verified with physical evidence. Their water-memory documents the Deepwell Confluence's aquifer behavior across spans of time that the Khephari's structural records, the Khenari's death-ledgers, and the Pyramid's construction histories all postdate. When disputes arise about the eastern desert's physical history — what the aquifer did during the Ninth Meridian's collapse, how the salt formations behaved during the desert civilization's shattering, where the original boundary of the Scorching Sands ran before it shifted — the Kethari's water-memory is the baseline record against which other accounts are checked.

This priority does not extend to all domains. On questions of death-administration, Khenari precedence is acknowledged. On questions of large-scale construction and structural integrity, Khephari expertise is deferred to. On questions of cosmic law, the Ushabti's authority is recognized by all parties, though what that recognition implies about compliance is a matter each people handles individually.

What Kethari precedence means in practice is: no major decision about eastern desert resources — water access, territorial claim, aquifer rights — is made by any of the other peoples without at minimum consulting the relevant Kethari coil-gathering. Whether the other peoples always follow Kethari counsel is a separate question. They ask. They always ask.

### Second Presence: The Khenari

The Khenari's position in the Desert Compact's order of precedence is more complicated than the Kethari's because the Khenari's sphere of authority cuts across geographic rather than temporal lines. Death-administration is not a territorial claim; it is a functional claim, and it applies wherever people die, which is everywhere.

The Khenari entered the eastern desert territories from Akh'enet, following the caravan routes that the Jackal Balance Court's legal authority followed. Their death-ledgers document the eastern territories from approximately the Salt Ledger Kingdoms period forward — not as long as the Kethari's water-memory but longer than the Khephari's formal presence in the region as a distinct people and much longer than the Ushabti's existence.

Their relationship with the Kethari is respectful and asymmetric in interesting ways: the Kethari are acknowledged as first in deep-time physical knowledge, but the Khenari are acknowledged as authoritative in the recording and legal processing of deaths, including deaths of Kethari. This creates a situation that neither people discusses openly but both navigate carefully: the Kethari know that when their community members die, the Khenari are the appropriate administrators of that death. They accept this not because Khenari authority was imposed on them but because their own philosophical framework — water-memory verifies by physical evidence; death-records require witnesses and formalization that their own tradition does not provide — supports it. The desert does not lie, and neither do the ledgers.

The Khenari's relationship with the Khephari carries a different weight. The Khephari who built the Pyramid's approach roads died in the course of that construction — some in accidents, some in circumstances that the death-ledgers of the relevant period record with careful precision that suggests the administering Khenari priests were aware they were documenting something that would be contested. These deaths are in the ledgers. The ledgers are maintained. Whether they will ever be formally presented to an institution capable of acknowledging their contents is a question the Khenari hold open with the patience that centuries of death-administration produces.

### Third Presence: The Khephari

The Khephari's position in the Desert Compact is, in some respects, the most paradoxical. They are the builders — the people whose labor produced the most visible physical transformation of the eastern desert landscape in the form of the Pyramid's approach infrastructure and the road networks that made the corridor navigable. Their contribution is literally the ground other peoples stand on. And yet in the Desert Compact's order of precedence, they are third.

This is not a slight. The Khephari themselves understand it as accurate. The Kethari were there first. The Khenari administer what the Khephari's labor sometimes produces. The Khephari are the makers, not the custodians. Makers have authority over what they make; custodians have authority over what persists. In the long timescales that the Desert Compact operates on, persistence is more fundamental than making.

What the Khephari hold in the Compact that the other peoples do not is structural authority: the assessment of what can be built, what will stand, what is sound. When the Pyramid's lower courts began showing signs of structural degradation — the breach that the Ushabti now monitor from inside and the Khenari document from the death-ledger evidence of what falls when structural failures produce casualties — the Khephari engineers' assessment of the cause was shared with both peoples as a matter of course. The Pyramid was built by Khephari labor; Khephari engineers are the appropriate authority on its structural health. This is not contested by any party in the Compact.

The Khephari's seismic knowledge, which now includes the crystal-resonance data they have been tracking in the western road foundations, is the element of their Compact contribution that has most recently become relevant beyond structural maintenance. What they are detecting in the west is not a structural problem they know how to assess; it is something new. Their willingness to share this data with the Compact's other members, rather than treating it as proprietary engineering knowledge, reflects the Compact's fundamental orientation: the eastern peoples share what they know when what they know affects territory and peoples beyond their immediate concern.

### The New Presence: The Ushabti

The Ushabti are the Desert Compact's most recent members — recent in the only sense that matters to a Compact that spans millennia, which is that they do not predate the institution that made them, and the Solar Court that made them is far younger than the oldest Kethari water-memory. They are, in the Compact's terms, newcomers. They are also, in the Compact's terms, operating without institutional sponsorship, since the Solar Court has dissolved, which puts them in a position that the Compact has not previously accommodated.

The question of whether the Ushabti are a people or an institutional artifact of the Solar Court is the most actively debated question in the Desert Compact's current period. The Kethari position, held by the eldest coil-gathering members, is patient: time will tell. A people who persist without an institution for long enough become a people in their own right, regardless of origin. The Ushabti have been operating without Solar Court direction for long enough that the Kethari are beginning to regard the question as functionally resolved, though they have not said so formally.

The Khenari position is characteristically ledger-based: the Ushabti are entered in the death-ledgers not as institutional records — their existence does not appear in the Solar Court's formal records in the Khenari category — but as the entries they generate when they are destroyed in service of cosmic law. These entries exist. Entries require a subject. A subject with a death-ledger entry is, for Khenari purposes, a person. The Khenari have therefore treated the Ushabti as people since the first time a Khenari mnemonist administered the Ninth Descent for an Ushabti destroyed in battle. Whether the Ushabti consider Khenari death-administration applicable to them is a question that has produced the most interesting long-form discussion in the Desert Compact's recent history (see `khenari_ushabti_jurisdiction_lore.md`).

The Khephari position is practical and warm: the Ushabti maintain the Pyramid's approach routes, which the Khephari built and consider, in some ancestral sense, their responsibility. The Ushabti who patrol those roads and repair the structural elements that the Khephari's descendants are no longer present to maintain are doing work that the Khephari regard as honorable. You respect the people who take care of what you built.

The Ushabti's own position on their place in the Desert Compact is characteristically careful: they do not claim precedence they have not earned, they do not dispute precedence that the other peoples' longer presence supports, and they observe — as they observe most things — the dynamics among the Compact's other members with the attention of people who are building the case-law of their own autonomous judgment in real time, one interaction at a time.

## The Compact in Practice

The Desert Compact does not meet in any formal sense. Its operation is visible in the pattern of deferences and consultations that occur when the four peoples' concerns intersect, which is regularly and without fanfare.

When a Khephari engineering team assesses a road segment in the eastern corridor, they send word to the nearest Khenari community about any deaths that occurred in the assessment process. When a Kethari coil-gathering makes a determination about aquifer access that affects the Pyramid's water supply, they inform the Ushabti who monitor the Pyramid's lower courts. When the Khenari's death-ledgers document casualties in a region where the Khephari have structural concerns about road integrity, the Khephari engineers include the casualty data in their load-bearing calculations. When an Ushabti invokes cosmic law in a proceeding that affects Kethari territorial claims, the Ushabti's variance record for that judgment includes the Kethari's perspective, because the Ushabti have decided that judgments affecting people require those people's account to be part of the case record.

This is not written down. It does not need to be. Four peoples sharing a desert for long enough learn each other's ways. The Compact's operation is visible not in documents but in behavior — in what the four peoples do when their concerns intersect, and in the quality of the outcomes that this pattern of mutual deference and consultation produces compared to the outcomes produced by human institutions that operate without it.

The eastern desert is, by any physical measure, a challenging environment. The four peoples who share it have sustained their communities and their knowledge across timescales that most human institutions cannot imagine. This is not coincidence.
