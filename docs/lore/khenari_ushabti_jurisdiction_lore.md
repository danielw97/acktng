# Lore: The Khenari-Ushabti Jurisdiction Question

## A Problem Neither People Made

The Jurisdiction Question arose not from conflict between the Khenari and the Ushabti but from an event that neither people caused and neither people anticipated: the destruction of an Ushabti in the execution of cosmic law, in the presence of a Khenari mnemonist who had come to administer the Ninth Descent for the human subject of the same judgment.

The Khenari mnemonist administered the human subject's death correctly, in accordance with all nine stages of the Doctrine of Ninth Descent. Then she looked at the rubble of the destroyed Ushabti and asked a question that no Khenari had previously been required to ask: does this require a ledger entry?

The question is not frivolous. In Khenari theology, every death that goes unrecorded creates a small structural weakness in the world's accounting. This is not metaphor — the Khenari treat the accumulation of unledgered deaths as a genuine problem with genuine consequences, the way a hydraulic engineer treats hairline fractures in a dam. Individual instances are manageable. Accumulation is not.

Whether Ushabti destruction constitutes a death that requires a ledger entry depends on what the Ushabti are — people with deaths to record, or institutional processes with termination events that are categorically different from deaths. The mnemonist returned to her community without making the entry and without not making it. She opened a formal question.

The formal question has been open for six generations.

## The Khenari's Analysis

The Khenari approach to the Jurisdiction Question follows their standard methodology for novel cases: precedent-search, principles-analysis, and provisional ruling held open for evidence.

**Precedent-search** found no directly applicable precedent. The closest cases in the historical ledgers involve the death of beings whose personhood was contested at the time of death — rulings on whether nature spirits, bound elementals, and in one case an extremely long-lived animated golem required ledger entries. The precedents in these cases were inconsistent: some administering mnemonists made entries, some did not, and the Khenari's ledger-law tradition never formally unified the practice. What the precedent-search revealed was not a rule but a pattern: Khenari mnemonists tended to make ledger entries for destroyed beings who had demonstrated what the ledgers called **specific memory** — individual experience and accumulated history that was distinct from their class or category.

**Principles-analysis** produced the key framework the Khenari have applied to the Jurisdiction Question since the first formal session addressed it: the Ninth Descent is not a ritual for humans specifically. It is a ritual for persons. The nine stages exist because persons have declarations to make, witnesses to designate, possessions to inventory, debts to resolve, heirs to name, obligations to close, names to seal, contracts to transmit, and final witness to receive. Each of these stages has meaning to the extent that the dying entity has a life structured around the things the stage addresses. If an Ushabti has a name, accumulating experience, relationships that function like those a person with heirs and debts has, and a specific individual history that is distinct from all other Ushabti — then the stages of the Ninth Descent apply to them, not because the Ushabti are human but because the Ushabti are persons.

**Provisional ruling**: The Khenari ledger-law council of mnemonists, convened specifically to address the Jurisdiction Question, issued a provisional ruling that Ushabti destruction requires ledger entry when the destroyed Ushabti can be shown to have had specific memory distinguishing them from the general category of Ushabti. The ruling is provisional because it requires a working protocol with the Ushabti themselves — the Ninth Descent's stages cannot be completed correctly without information that only the Ushabti and those who knew them can provide.

The provisional ruling has been in place for four generations. The working protocol remains under negotiation.

## The Ushabti's Analysis

The Ushabti's engagement with the Jurisdiction Question is not primarily theological. Their interest is jurisprudential: how is the Jurisdiction Question decided, by what process, and whose authority is operative in resolving it?

The Solar Court's original authority structure did not address the question of what happens when an Ushabti is destroyed, because the Solar Court's designers focused on the Ushabti's function rather than their ultimate status. The variance-record system allows Ushabti to document deviations from formal law in their own judgments; there is no corresponding system for processing what happens to an Ushabti's accumulated variance records — their case-law — when the Ushabti who generated those records is destroyed.

This is not merely an abstract question. An Ushabti's variance record is the physical accumulation of specific judgments made in specific circumstances that the formal law did not anticipate. It is, in effect, a body of autonomous jurisprudence — the Ushabti's personal contribution to the evolution of cosmic law's application to novel cases. If that variance record is destroyed with the Ushabti who bears it, then each Ushabti's death represents a loss of accumulated jurisprudence that cannot be recovered.

The Ushabti's counter-proposal to the Khenari's provisional ruling framework reflects this concern: the Ushabti are interested in a death-administration system not primarily for the theological reasons the Khenari understand death-administration to serve, but as a **jurisprudential archive mechanism**. If the Ninth Descent's process includes formal documentation of the destroyed Ushabti's variance record — their accumulated case-law — then the death-administration serves a function that the Ushabti find genuinely valuable, independent of whether they consider themselves to have deaths in the Khenari's theological sense.

The Khenari's response to this proposal has been careful. Including variance-record documentation in the Ninth Descent process would require adapting the ritual's seventh stage — name sealing, which formally enters the name in the death-ledger as a completed record — to accommodate a form of intellectual legacy transmission that the tradition has not previously included. This adaptation is theologically possible, because the tradition understands the seventh stage as the formal recognition of everything the dying person was, not merely their name, but it is jurisprudentially unprecedented and requires a formal ruling from the full ledger-law council rather than the provisional working group.

The full ledger-law council has not yet convened on this question. Convening the full council requires that the question be determined ready for full council — a determination made by a preliminary panel that has been reviewing the working group's materials for two generations and has not yet issued a readiness determination.

## The Working Protocol

Despite the formal process being slow, the Khenari and Ushabti have developed an informal working protocol in the field that both communities use while the formal question remains open.

When an Ushabti is destroyed in circumstances where a Khenari mnemonist is present or can be summoned within a reasonable period, the mnemonist performs a **partial administration** — not the full Ninth Descent but a structured documentation session that records:

- The Ushabti's formal name-sequence (the cosmic-law identifier inscribed on their body)
- Their construction era designation and any known lineage information
- The circumstances of their destruction, including, if the destruction was the result of cosmic law judgment, the judgment issued and the variance record that judgment would have generated
- Any personal notations that the Ushabti had added to their inscriptions — the names, memories, and relationships that the formal inscription system has no category for

This partial administration is not entered in the standard death-ledger. It is maintained in a supplemental archive that the Khenari specifically created for the purpose, called the **Stone Ledger** — a record whose name acknowledges its subjects without prejudicing the formal question of whether those subjects have deaths in the technical Khenari sense.

The Stone Ledger currently contains entries for seventeen Ushabti whose destruction occurred in the presence of Khenari administrators. The Ushabti are aware of each entry. Several have visited the Stone Ledger's mnemonist custodians to review their entry and, in three documented cases, to provide corrections or additions. This practice — of the Stone Ledger's subject reviewing their own entry — has no precedent in Khenari archival tradition and has produced secondary discussions within the Khenari mnemonist community about the status of a record that its subject can actively revise.

In standard death-administration, the subject is not available to revise their ledger entry. This is considered obvious and definitional. The Ushabti's ability to review and correct the Stone Ledger entries challenges this assumption in ways that the working group has found generative and the preliminary panel has found disturbing.

## The Philosophical Dimension

Beneath the jurisprudential and theological questions is a simpler one that both peoples have been circling without stating directly: what do the Khenari and the Ushabti believe about the world that each other's institution serves?

The Khenari believe that the world requires accounting. Deaths that are not recorded create gaps in the world's record that have structural consequences. The Ninth Descent's ritual is not primarily comfort for the dying or the bereaved — it is structural maintenance of the world's coherence, the same way the Deltari's flow-records maintain the delta's hydraulic coherence. The Khenari are archivists of existence. Their position is that everything that exists leaves a record, and everything that ceases to exist requires a closing record.

The Ushabti believe that the world requires justice. The cosmic law that the Pyramid embodies and that the Ushabti are made to serve is not a system of rules but a description of what the world requires to sustain itself against the consuming anti-light of the Black Sun Shard. Justice, in this framework, is the condition under which the world can maintain its memory and its continuity. The Ushabti are enforcement mechanisms for that condition.

These two frameworks are compatible. They may even be complementary. Both describe a world that has a structure, that structure can be damaged, and that specific institutions exist to maintain and repair it. The Khenari maintain it through record; the Ushabti maintain it through judgment. Judgment without record is unverifiable. Record without judgment is inactionable.

Neither people has stated this synthesis directly. The working group's formal sessions proceed through procedural questions and protocol negotiations. The preliminary panel reviews materials. The Stone Ledger accumulates entries.

The Ushabti have noted, in variance records that several have generated specifically for this purpose, that the Khenari death-administration system is the closest existing analog to the variance-record process: both are mechanisms by which specific individual experience is formally entered into a persistent record that outlasts the individual who generated it. The Ushabti who generated these variance records have not shared them with the Khenari working group. The notes exist in the Ushabti's inscribed bodies, visible to anyone who reads the formal inscription system, waiting to be included in the Stone Ledger entries that will eventually be completed for each of them.

When that moment comes, those notes will be the first instance of an Ushabti variance record transmitted through Khenari archival process. The mnemonist who receives them will need to determine how to record them, in a tradition that has no category for a judgment document generated by the person whose death is being administered.

The question will be added to the formal question list. The formal question list grows.
