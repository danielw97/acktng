# Lore: Kowloon

Extracted from `kowloon_plan.md`.

## Lore
### Before the Walls: Delta of Lanterns
Before Kowloon had a name, the delta was called the **Shallow Thousand**—a drowned lacework of stilts, prayer poles, tide farms, and contraband docks stretching across nine miles of alluvial mud where the Jhen River emptied into the Coppersalt Bay. Clan skiffs moved by moon and bell, not by law. Some villages paid tribute to river spirits, some to mercenary captains, and some to whichever gang controlled the grain bridges that season.

The delta's oldest inhabitants were the **Lampkeepers**, solitary hermits who maintained oil beacons on bamboo towers so that night fishermen could navigate the shifting channels. These beacons gave the delta its poetic name—the **Field of Lanterns**—and the hermits' tradition of tending light for strangers without asking payment or allegiance became the philosophical seed of Kowloon's later civic neutrality.

For a time, this patchwork of mutual exploitation worked. Then came the **Ashfall Monsoon**. For three consecutive years the rain turned black with volcanic grit from the Cinderteeth Mountains far to the northeast. Roofs collapsed under acidic sludge, canals choked withiteite calcium sediment, and fresh water went bitter with sulfur. Compacts failed, river feuds became famine wars, and an entire generation learned to sleep above floodline with a blade in hand.

The worst year was the third. A flash flood swallowed the village of Yen-Mak whole—six hundred souls drowned in a single night. The survivors crawled onto a basalt outcrop that never submerged, lit signal fires, and waited. When rescue came, it came from every direction: former enemies pulling the same ropes.

### The Neon Covenant
In the fourth year, six surviving houses convened on that same basalt outcrop, lit by storm lanterns and guarded by oathbound killers drawn from rival bloodlines. Their pact became the **Neon Covenant**—so named because the first copy was inked in phosphorescent squid dye that glowed under torchlight, proving it had not been altered in darkness.

The six houses each brought something irreplaceable to the table:
1. **House Tahn-Rei** (the Ferry Masters): controlled every navigable channel and knew the delta's shifting mud-maps by memory. They demanded control of harbor tariffs in exchange for opening all waterways.
2. **House Vao** (the Grain Wardens): maintained the largest surviving seed vaults and granary barges. They demanded central rationing authority during crisis years.
3. **House Ohn-Sek** (the Iron Hands): mercenary soldiers and wall-builders whose fortified compound had sheltered refugees from three clans during the worst floods. They demanded permanent command of city defenses.
4. **House Jien** (the Jade Clerks): scribes, accountants, and contract arbiters whose records had settled more disputes than any warlord's sword. They demanded authority over civil law and taxation.
5. **House Mei-Lao** (the Temple Wardens): healers, morticians, and keepers of ancestral shrines who had cremated plague dead without discrimination during the monsoon years. They demanded sanctuary jurisdiction and disaster authority.
6. **House Qo** (the Lantern Runners): descendants of the Lampkeepers who had evolved into couriers, mapmakers, and intelligence brokers. They demanded control of the city's communications, registry, and recall infrastructure.

The Covenant demanded three hard promises:
1. **One wall, one law:** every district inside the walls answers to civic statute, not clan retaliation. Any blood feud prosecuted inside the perimeter is treason.
2. **One ledger, one ration code:** grain, medicine, and timber are tracked centrally in shortage years. Hoarding is punishable by public inventory and forced sale.
3. **One square, final judgment:** disputes unresolved by magistrates are settled under public witness at the covenant stone. No private justice, no purchased verdicts, no appeals beyond the stone.

The new city was raised on terraces, drainage vaults, and reinforced underways. Old villages were dismantled plank by plank, and their timber was recycled into a rectangular plan that could be patrolled, supplied, and defended in any season. The basalt outcrop became the foundation of the central square—the covenant stone still visible beneath the flagstones.

### The Five Seats of Rule
Over generations, the six founding houses dissolved into five civic institutions as intermarriage, succession crises, and institutional evolution blurred bloodline boundaries. Modern Kowloon is governed by:

- **Harbor Syndics** (descended from House Tahn-Rei): regulate docks, tariffs, long-haul contracts, and the licensing of foreign vessels. Their offices occupy the eastern perimeter near the harbor gate, where syndic inspectors in blue sashes audit every cargo manifest before it crosses the threshold.
- **Jade Magistracy** (descended from House Jien, absorbing remnants of House Vao): tax authority, civil law, licensing, and emergency rationing. The Magistracy's jade seal—an actual carved jade block weighing forty pounds—must physically stamp any proclamation of martial law. It takes two magistrates to carry it, ensuring no single official can invoke emergency powers alone.
- **Temple Circle** (descended from House Mei-Lao): healing houses, funerary rites, disaster sanctuaries, and the arbitration of oaths. Temple healers wear undyed linen and answer to no secular authority while treating patients, a privilege granted by the original Covenant and never revoked.
- **Wardens of Iron** (descended from House Ohn-Sek): walls, gate discipline, armed response, and the training of city militia. The Wardens maintain a strict rotation—no officer commands the same gate for more than one season, preventing any single commander from cultivating loyalty at a chokepoint.
- **Courier Lantern Office** (descended from House Qo): registry archives, post routes, recall stone administration, and the maintenance of the city's signal-lantern network. The CLO also operates the census, tracking every soul inside the walls by name, trade, and district. Their runners carry brass lantern-badges that grant passage through any gate at any hour.

No seat rules alone. Each can censure another, and all five are bound by tribunal record kept in triplicate on oiled vellum. The city believes balance is safer than trust.

### Colors of Power, Memory, and Street Life
Kowloon's colors are not decoration—they are civic shorthand born from disaster, readable at a glance by anyone who lives inside the walls:
- **Crimson (`@@R`)** marks emergency lanes, legal notices, execution authority, and the painted boundaries of martial-law zones. When crimson flags fly from the watchtowers, citizens know to clear the streets.
- **Jade (`@@G`)** marks permits, public granaries, magistrate offices, and the doors of licensed advocates. A jade ribbon on a merchant's stall means the Magistracy has audited their weights and measures within the season.
- **Gold (`@@y`)** marks licensed market corridors, bonded merchants, and the frontages of guild-certified craftsmen. Gold lacquer on a doorframe means the owner has posted bond against fraud.
- **Blue (`@@B`)** marks pumps, sluices, dock chains, storm engineering, and the Harbor Syndics' jurisdiction. Blue lanterns along the eastern wall guide ships through the harbor channel at night.
- **Violet (`@@p`)** marks licensed night markets, occult exchanges, private guild salons, and the discreet entrances of fortune-tellers and charm-sellers. Violet is the color of sanctioned ambiguity—things done under violet light are legal but not discussed in daylight.
- **Light-cyan (`@@a`)** marks rainwater routes, glass beacons, floodline indicators, and the Courier Lantern Office's relay stations. During monsoon season, cyan paint on a wall tells you how high the water rose last year.

Citizens read color as quickly as text. Visitors who ignore it usually pay in coin, blood, or both.

### The Heart of the City: Execution Square
The **Crimson-Jade Execution Square** sits over the original covenant basalt and functions as recall anchor, legal stage, and political pressure valve. Every proclamation of war, ration, plague response, and debt amnesty has been spoken from its elevated stones. The flagstones are ancient basalt—darker than the surrounding granite—and citizens swear they can feel a low hum through their boots when standing at the center, the residual vibration of oaths two centuries old.

The **Executioner** stationed there is a constitutional office, not a faction soldier. The post is occupied by a figure known only by title—never by name, never by face, which is hidden behind an iron half-mask forged in the shape of a snarling river dragon. The office can be activated by tribunal seal when city peace is broken: assassination inside sanctuary wards, sabotage of gate machinery, theft from emergency reserves, or riot during flood alarm. The office's public neutrality is terrifying by design: everyone fears it, so everyone obeys the square. Children in Kowloon grow up hearing the saying: *"The Executioner has no enemies, because the Executioner has no friends."*

### District Memory and Daily Rhythm
Kowloon appears rigid on maps, but each district carries older identities beneath the rectangular plan:

- **Northern lanes** remember the village registrars and ferry clerks who became the modern post and administration spine. The Courier Lantern Office headquarters sits here, its rooftop relay station visible from every district. Residents of the northern quarter tend toward quiet industriousness—clerks, archivists, and cartographers who speak in precise sentences and keep meticulous personal ledgers.
- **Western markets** descended from tarp bazaars that once floated from pier to pier with tide levels. Even now, western merchants display a superstitious habit of hanging their wares on hooks rather than laying them on tables, an echo of the old floating-stall tradition. The air here smells of cured leather, lamp oil, and the sharp tang of fresh-forged steel from the smithing lanes.
- **Eastern corridors** house ritual specialists, arcane brokers, and the city's only licensed thaumaturgic exchange—a place where spent spell components are recycled, enchantment residues are assessed for salvage value, and hedge-wizards can purchase reagents without the stigma of black-market dealing. The Temple Circle maintains a heavy presence here, partly for healing services and partly to monitor any magical practice that might threaten the city's structural wards.
- **Southern terraces** hold barracks, warehouses, caravan yards, and the sprawling inn district built on former flood refuges. The southern quarter never fully sheds its frontier character—it is louder, rougher, and more tolerant of strangers than the rest of the city. Warden patrols are heavier here, not because crime is worse, but because the population turns over faster.

At dawn, bronze bells signal pump inspections and gate inventories. Midday belongs to contracts, provisioning, and tribunal filings. Dusk shifts toward tavern diplomacy, courier exchange, and quiet black-market whispers in violet-lit alleys. After midnight, only Warden patrols, Courier Lantern runners, and those with business best left undescribed move through the streets.

### Why Adventurers Matter Here
Kowloon welcomes all levels because danger around the delta never truly ended. New adventurers escort supply trains, clear drainage tunnels clogged with monsoon debris and feral creatures, and guard shrine routes between the city and outlying temple sites. Veteran companies negotiate high-risk salvage rights beyond the gate roads, hunt organized raider bands in the surrounding wetlands, and recover lost covenant relics from submerged ruins that surface unpredictably as the river shifts its channels.

The city's service geometry reflects this practical ethos: key shops cluster near traversal spines so adventurers can resupply without navigating the full grid, interiors are single-access for auditability and theft prevention, and quartermaster logistics are positioned near the central recall nexus so recovering parties can rearm fast. Kowloon does not romanticize adventurers—it processes them with the same brisk efficiency it applies to cargo manifests and tax filings. But it pays fairly, heals without prejudice, and never asks where you came from, only what you can carry.

### Connection to the Saltglass Reach and Mafdet

Kowloon's Jade Magistracy has identified Mafdet's Ledger House notation as a relative of their own record-keeping system — both descended from the **Littoral Branch** of the Keepers of Measure's combinatorial writing system. The Jade Clerks' independent reconstruction of volumetric-legal Measure Script aligns more closely with the Littoral Branch than with the inland system, suggesting that the trade documents they used as source material may have been maritime in origin — carried to Kowloon by coastal routes rather than overland caravans.

This discovery has prompted the Jade Magistracy to fund research expeditions seeking Littoral Branch source documents at **Measure's Rest**, the Saltglass Reach Wardens' cairn tablet library. The expeditions are politically sensitive: the Reach Wardens demand payment in information (they want to know what the Jade Clerks are building with the data), and Mafdet's Ledger Houses view the Jade Clerks' interest as a potential challenge to their commercial monopoly on maritime legal notation.

If confirmed, a historical maritime trade link between Mafdet and Kowloon's delta ports would rewrite corridor commercial history. It would mean that the Keepers' measurement civilization reached the sea in two directions — east to Mafdet and (through unknown intermediaries) to Kowloon's delta — and that the two maritime adaptations evolved independently for centuries before modern scholars noticed the family resemblance.

Kowloon's **Neon Covenant** principle of "one ledger, one ration code" echoes Mafdet's **Strandline Compact** principle of "dual attestation" — both reflect the same underlying conviction that documentary precision is the foundation of civic trust. The institutional parallels are striking: both cities were founded by practical compacts forged during crisis (Kowloon's Ashfall Monsoon, Mafdet's Fracture Era), both cities' governance divides power among three-to-five blocs that prevent any single institution from dominating, and both cities treat measurement and documentation as the primary instruments of social order.

### Enemies, Tensions, and Rumors
Peace in Kowloon is durable, not gentle. Ongoing tensions include:

- **River Corsairs** under the command of a figure known as **Captain Blacktide Shen**, a former Tahn-Rei scion who was exiled for smuggling during a rationing year. Shen's corsairs test gate convoy timings and warehouse patrol schedules with probing raids, never committing to a full assault but bleeding the city's logistics budget through constant harassment. The Harbor Syndics have posted a bounty, but Shen knows the channels better than anyone alive.
- **Salt Court Exiles**, led by the self-styled **Duchess Yuen Ahi**, the last heir of a branch of House Vao that was purged during a succession crisis three decades ago. The Salt Court funds unrest through debt rings and protection rackets in the southern quarter, exploiting the transient population's vulnerability. Yuen Ahi claims the Jade Magistracy's authority is illegitimate because the merger that absorbed House Vao was never ratified by all six founding houses—a legal argument that, inconveniently, may have merit.
- **Ash Cult cells** that worship the Cinderteeth eruptions as divine judgment and believe the Ashfall Monsoon was interrupted, not ended. Their leader, known only as **The Ember Speaker**, preaches that the Covenant is an act of hubris—humanity defying the mountains' verdict—and that true salvation requires the city's destruction and a return to the "honest poverty" of the pre-Covenant delta. Cult cells have been caught sabotaging floodworks and contaminating water reserves, though never in sufficient numbers to cause catastrophic damage. The Temple Circle considers them heretics; the Wardens consider them terrorists. Notably, Ash Cult ideology has found scattered adherents in Rakuen as well — where two centuries of disaster stacking have given the Cinderteeth's "divine judgment" framing a darker plausibility. The Ember Speaker is believed to have sent emissaries south through the Eccentric Woodland, though contact with Rakuen's cult presence (if any) has not been confirmed by either city's intelligence services.
- **Ledger wars** between legal syndics and contraband financiers over shipping control. The most powerful smuggling syndicate, the **Jade Eels**, operates through a network of shell companies and bribed customs clerks. Their leader has never been identified—some believe it is a sitting member of the Harbor Syndics, which would make the corruption structural rather than criminal.

Persistent rumors speak of a sealed undercity cistern beneath the central quadrants, accessible only through collapsed drainage tunnels that predate the Covenant's rectangular plan. According to the stories, the cistern contains the **Black Ledger**—the original unredacted census of the six founding houses, including birth records, debt tallies, and blood-oath registries that were supposedly destroyed when the houses merged into civic institutions. If the Black Ledger exists, it could invalidate noble titles, expose forged lineage claims, prove that the Covenant was signed under duress by at least two houses, or reveal that one of the founding houses never actually consented at all. Every faction in Kowloon would kill to find it—or to ensure it stays buried.

### Gates and the Promise of Return
Kowloon's four heavy gates each bear a name and a character:

- **The Jade Gate** (north) opens toward the highland trade routes and the administrative post roads. It is the quietest gate, favored by couriers and diplomatic envoys. A carved jade lintel depicts a scale in balance.
- **The Iron Gate** (south) faces the caravan roads and the lawless wetland frontier. It is the busiest and most heavily guarded, with Warden checkpoints both inside and outside the threshold. An iron portcullis supplements the wooden doors.
- **The Lantern Gate** (west) opens toward the inland farming communes and pilgrim paths. It is decorated with hundreds of small oil lanterns hung by travelers who made it home safely—a tradition dating back to the Lampkeepers.
- **The Tide Gate** (east) faces the harbor and the open water of Coppersalt Bay. It is the widest gate, built to accommodate cargo wagons, and the only gate with a resident customs inspector who never sleeps (rumor says the Syndics keep three inspectors in rotation, but only one badge).

All four gates symbolize a hard civic creed: **open to trade, closed to chaos**. They are reset secure by policy, inspected by shift, and opened by measured protocol rather than emotion. Inside, movement is fast and practical; outside, risk is assumed.

For battered travelers, recall to the Execution Square is more than convenience—it is the city's oldest promise: if you can still stand, Kowloon will receive you, count you, provision you, judge you if needed, and send you back into the rain.

### Festivals and Superstitions
Kowloon observes three major festivals:
- **Lantern Remembrance** (spring): citizens hang lit lanterns on every available hook and pole in memory of the Lampkeepers and the drowned village of Yen-Mak. The city glows so brightly that ships at sea use it for navigation.
- **Ledger Day** (midsummer): the Jade Magistracy opens its books for public audit. Any citizen may challenge a tax assessment. Disputes are settled by sunset or escalated to the covenant stone. Vendors sell ink-stained rice cakes shaped like jade seals.
- **Ashfall Vigil** (autumn): a solemn night when the city extinguishes all lights for one hour at midnight, remembering the years of black rain. When the lights return, the Temple Circle leads a processional chant and the Executioner strikes the covenant stone three times with an iron rod—once for the dead, once for the living, once for those not yet born.

Common superstitions include: never count your coins under violet light (invites the Jade Eels' attention), always enter a gate with your right foot first (ensures you will leave alive), and never speak the Ember Speaker's true name aloud (those who claim to know it tend to disappear).
