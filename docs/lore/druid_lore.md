# Lore: The Druid Tradition

## Overview

Druids are the inheritors of the **original relationship** between practitioners and power — not one tradition among many, but the baseline condition that every other tradition developed away from. Before mana was conceptualized as a separable internal reserve. Before the Solar Court's Seal College developed cosmological infrastructure as a power source. Before any institution gave practitioners a framework for accessing the living world without offering something of themselves in return. There was the vitality exchange: give life, receive response. The living world has never worked any other way.

Every mana-using tradition in the five-city network represents a development away from this original state. The arcane schools found ways to accumulate internal power reserves — to charge and discharge mana without paying bodily cost each time. The cleric tradition connected practitioners to cosmological infrastructure maintained at institutional scale. These are genuine innovations. The druid tradition is not. It is the unchanged original, practiced by the tradition that never found a way to avoid the cost and, eventually, concluded that finding a way to avoid it was the wrong goal.

Their spells do not draw on mana. They draw on the caster's own life force — a practice called the **Overgrowth Doctrine** by those who theorize it, and called simply *the cost* by those who live it. The tradition understands this not as a limitation but as the most honest possible description of what power is: accumulated vitality, returning to the system from which it came.

Three ancient druidic orders — the **Everkeepers**, the **forest druids of the Withered Depths**, and the **Cairn-Keepers** — were themselves already developed specializations of something more primordial. They had named doctrines, stone circles, and institutional structures (pre-civic but structured). Before them were the unnamed practitioners of the pre-city era, and before those, the original undifferentiated baseline: anyone who contacted the living world and tried to draw on it, and discovered the vitality exchange because there is nothing else to discover. None of these orders survived the civic era intact. The tradition's modern practitioners are the products of a fractured reconstruction — but the underlying mechanics they are reconstructing never changed, because the living world has not changed.

The druid lineage progresses through four stages: the foundational **Druid** class, the two intermediate remort paths of **Thornwarden** (martial-enforcement specialization) and **Wildspeaker** (ecological-communion specialization), and the advanced **Hierophant** designation available only to those who have mastered both remort traditions. The Hierophant represents the rarest outcome in the tradition: a practitioner who has fully internalized both the Thornwarden's capacity for physical dominion and the Wildspeaker's capacity for living-world dialogue.

## The Foundational Druid Class

### Constitution as Covenant

The druid tradition's primary attribute is **constitution** — not intelligence, not wisdom, not strength, but the raw physical capacity to endure cost. This is not incidental. Every other casting tradition in the five-city network draws on internal reserves that are distinct from the body's structural integrity: a wizard's mana is not their blood; a cleric's connection to cosmological infrastructure is not their flesh. A druid's casting resource is their life. A practitioner who enters the tradition with poor physical endurance is a practitioner who has very little to give, and the living world does not extend credit.

The tradition understands this clearly and trains for it explicitly. Foundational druid training emphasizes physical conditioning alongside ritual knowledge — not because druids are fighters (though the later Thornwarden path develops martial capacity), but because the practitioner who cannot sustain physical hardship cannot sustain the Overgrowth curve. A druid who collapses from exhaustion before completing a sustained casting sequence has not failed magically; they have failed constitutionally. The distinction is important: it means the training target is the body, not the mind. A more intelligent druid who has not built their physical endurance is a less capable druid than a less intelligent one who has.

The Exchange School makes this point bluntly: you cannot give what you do not have. The Covenant School's formulation is gentler — the living world can only commune with what is present, and a body that has not built sufficient presence has nothing to offer — but the practical training recommendation is the same. Build constitution first.

What the tradition calls "constitution as covenant" also carries a historical claim: mana is not the original condition. The arcane schools built their frameworks around mana — a separable internal reserve that could be accumulated and spent without the body being the medium of cost — but this was an innovation developed in an era when forest practitioners had already been operating on the vitality exchange for longer than those institutions had existed. The Solar Court's Seal College, earlier still, had found a way to access power through external cosmological infrastructure rather than direct bodily cost. Every tradition that came after the primordial baseline found something to interpose between the practitioner and the exchange. The druid tradition's CON-primary design is not an inability to make that interposition. It is the maintained position — settled through the tradition's full historical arc — that what you interpose changes what you are doing in ways that the living world itself recognizes.

### The Spell Progression: A Training Arc

The foundational druid's spell progression from level three to ninety is not a linear escalation of power but a structured teaching arc in which each new ability is a lesson about the tradition's principles as much as a functional tool.

**The initial pairing (level 3)** is philosophically deliberate: *thornlash* and *sap mend* are learned simultaneously. The first offensive spell and the first healing spell arrive together. The tradition's opening lesson is that the capacity to harm and the capacity to restore are not different categories of knowledge — they are the same knowledge applied to different purposes. Both draw equally on the practitioner's constitution (growth value 3 each). The practitioner who can heal can harm; the practitioner who can harm can heal. The training programs that introduce these spells in the wrong order, or treat them as separately tracked competencies, are considered by the tradition to be missing the foundational point.

**Early acquisition (levels 5–20)** builds the practitioner's vocabulary across all four functional areas in parallel: defense (*barkskin* at 5, *roothold* at 9, *thornhide* at 14), offense (*nettlesting* at 6, *rockburst* at 10, *wither* at 16, *briar volley* at 20), sensory (*forestsight* at 7, *sense life* at 11, *know terrain* at 15), and control (*tangling roots* at 8, *sap strength* at 18). The mending arc continues with *mend wounds* at 12 and *purge toxin* at 22.

The pattern of cross-category alternation in early acquisition is understood in the tradition as a deliberate teaching rhythm: a druid who specializes too early — who develops offensive capacity without contemporaneous defensive and sensory development — is building a practice that will fail under ecological pressure, because the living world does not separate threat from health any more than the druid's spell list does. A forest threatened by fungal blight is simultaneously a forest generating healing potential in its own immune response. Reading both at once is required.

**Substrate piercing** unlocks at level 20 as the tradition's first passive skill, accessible after one reincarnation. The name refers to a quality of tactile and kinesthetic attunement that develops in practitioners who have spent sustained time in Overgrowth: the body's contact with surfaces — soil, bark, stone — becomes informationally richer, as though the tissue itself has been calibrated by repeated HP exchange to read the living substrate beneath. A practitioner with substrate piercing active does not simply stand on earth; they stand in it, in the sense that the boundary between their physical body and the living material they contact has become partially permeable to information in both directions. The Root Covenant clan theology describes this as "reshaping the substrate that spells depend on" — a formulation that the Exchange School finds imprecise but recognizes as pointing at something real.

**Mid-range acquisition (levels 22–50)** deepens Overgrowth commitment. *Blight touch* at 25 is the first spell with a damage-over-time component (growth 5), introducing the practitioner to extended-duration effects that require the body to maintain a sustained low-level expenditure across multiple rounds rather than a single acute cost. *Tremor* at 32 and *creeping rot* at 38 continue the escalation in both earth and poison domains (growth 5 each). *Verdant mend* at 35 provides the first healing spell capable of addressing significant combat damage without requiring the practitioner to be fully out of Overgrowth first — a practical necessity given that mid-range combat routinely involves HP expenditure that low-growth heals cannot address quickly.

*Ironpine vigor* at 36 is the first buff that references a specific ecological heritage: the ironpine's distinctive structural density. A practitioner who has developed genuine attunement with ironpine stands — through the Everkeepers' old territories or through the northern routes maintained by road wardens — finds this spell more reliable than one who knows the ironpine only abstractly. This is a general principle of the tradition's buff spells: they work better when the practitioner has actual experiential acquaintance with what they are drawing on.

**High-range acquisition (levels 55–90)** commits the practitioner fully to the deep Overgrowth curve. *Fissure* at 46 (growth 6) and *fungal eruption* at 55 (growth 6, AoE) establish the first truly heavy offensive expenditures. *Quake* at 60 and *upheaval* at 85 (both growth 6 and 7 respectively) are the highest-growth AoE spells in the catalogue — room-scale earth disruptions that cost substantially more than any single-target attack. A practitioner casting *upheaval* has committed significant vitality to the act and is operating well into Overgrowth by the time the spell completes.

*Rot* at 75 and *creeping blight* at 78 (growth 7 and 5 respectively) represent the poison doctrine's endpoint: *rot* is the most powerful single-target poison effect in the catalogue, a direct acceleration of biological decay that does not try to be anything other than what it is. *Creeping blight* pairs it with the tradition's deepest stat-drain combination (STR and CON simultaneously), reflecting the understanding that what decay does is consume precisely the things that sustained the living system.

*Sap transfusion* at 80 (growth 3) stands out: despite arriving at high level, it has one of the lowest growth values in the catalogue. This is intentional. The spell is not powerful in the sense that it draws heavily on the practitioner's connection to the living world; it is powerful in the sense that it does exactly what it does, directly, with minimal conversion loss. The tradition keeps it accessible specifically because there are moments in deep Overgrowth when the practitioner needs to give HP to someone else and cannot afford to also pay heavy growth costs for the privilege of doing so.

*Living bridge* at 90 is the final spell, and it arrives as the capstone of the utility arc (*forestsight* at 7 → *sense life* at 11 → *passage* at 24 → *greensight* at 42 → *rootway* at 58 → *call of the wild* at 72 → *living bridge* at 90). A portal sustained through root and mycelial networks, usable from anywhere the practitioner has sufficient living-world contact, it is the tradition's statement about what full attainment means: you are not simply a practitioner who can fight in the forest, or heal wounds the forest might sustain. You have become a node in the living network itself.

### The Overgrowth Accumulation Curve

The most important practical fact about foundational druid combat is that the Overgrowth state is not just a warning indicator — it is the tradition's core power mechanic turned against the practitioner. When a druid's Overgrowth counter is elevated, their druid-school damage spells hit harder (the living world's response amplified by the depth of commitment), but each subsequent spell costs proportionally more HP and the Overgrowth counter climbs further. The curve is self-reinforcing in both directions: deeper Overgrowth produces more damage output and accelerates the practitioner's consumption simultaneously.

The combat decisions this creates distinguish druid play from all other traditions. A wizard manages a depleting mana pool and eventually cannot cast. A druid manages a depleting HP pool that is also their life total — and the deeper they go into Overgrowth, the more lethal each subsequent decision becomes. The practitioner who pushes to 30% HP while in heavy Overgrowth and commits to another *fissure* is not simply spending resources; they are spending the margin between combat effectiveness and death.

The Exchange School's training programs spend significant time on what they call **commitment calibration**: developing the practitioner's ability to judge, in live combat conditions, exactly how far into Overgrowth the current situation warrants. This is not a formula. It is a trained judgment about probability and cost, and it develops through practice and consequence. The Covenant School notes that this judgment cannot be separated from the practitioner's relationship with the living world they are drawing on — that the most reliable guide to how far is too far is the living world's own feedback, accessible to practitioners who have developed sufficient attunement to read it in real time.

Both schools agree on the recovery principle: a druid who manages Overgrowth well spends combat moving up and down the curve in controlled increments, not climbing to a peak and hoping the enemy dies before the HP budget runs out. The practitioner who treats Overgrowth as a one-way escalation ramp is practicing the doctrine of the Withered Depths order's final generation — burning everything on the assumption that the thing being contained will yield before the practitioner does. History records how that ended.

## The Primordial Condition: Before the Named Orders

Before the Everkeepers named their doctrine, before the unnamed order of the Withered Depths created its first stone circle, before the Cairn-Keepers began placing the honored dead in north-facing stone niches, the vitality exchange existed and was being practiced. It was not a doctrine. It was a discovery — the same discovery every practitioner makes when they first contact the living world and find that it does not give without receiving. The forest is not a mana pool. It does not fill up. It is in continuous exchange with everything living in it, and practitioners who draw on it successfully are those who enter that exchange rather than trying to extract from it.

The practitioners who first understood this had no institutional framework for it, no vocabulary for theorizing it, no master lineages to trace it through. They had the living world and the results of trying different things. This is still how the druid tradition works at its foundation, which is one of the reasons the reconstruction effort — operating without master-to-student transmission for the first time in the tradition's history — was able to produce anything at all. The original method is simple enough to rediscover. What is difficult to rediscover is everything the named orders built on top of it, the two thousand years of refinement between the first forest practitioner who noticed that giving something got a response and the last Everkeeper who planted a ring calculated to hold sky-architecture load.

### The Black Sun Shard and the Forest's First Crisis

The era called the Primordial Age in scholarly accounts includes a cosmic event whose effects reached far beyond the desert where it fell: the **Black Sun Shard**, a fragment of anti-light that consumed names, memories, and certainties. Crops failed under clear skies. The dead rose remembering wrong lives. Households forgot their bloodlines. The Shard's erosive properties did not stop at the desert's edge — memory erosion is not bounded by geography — and what the Shard unmade was not physical matter but accumulated record.

The forest is one of the world's primary archives of accumulated record.

For forest practitioners, the Shard's effects were an attack on the substrate they relied on. The living world's continuous broadcast of its own state — the signal that druid practice reads and draws on — is a form of memory. Growth rings record years. Root systems encode the shapes of previous disturbances. The mycelial networks that link trees across distances carry chemical records of what the forest has survived. When the Shard's erosion reached the northern forests, it did not kill trees outright. It confused them. Growth patterns that had encoded decades of adaptation became noise. Root systems that had learned the shapes of seasonal drought forgot what they knew. The forest's self-knowledge — its living archive — was being consumed without any visible cause.

Forest practitioners had no framework for addressing cosmic forces. They could not contain the Shard. They could not calculate it. What the vitality exchange gives its practitioners, and only its practitioners, is the capacity to deepen rather than contain: go further underground, where the Shard's anti-light reached less. Propagate the living archive into mycelial networks at root-depth rather than in the vulnerable surface canopy. Commit more vitality to the exchange so that the record could be preserved against the erosion. Not a shield. Not a calculation. A strategic retreat into biological complexity, making the system being threatened too distributed and too deep to be fully consumed.

This deepening is the origin of the Wildspeaker path's underground mobility — the *rootway* and *living bridge* spells whose mechanism runs through mycelial and root networks rather than surface geography. The tradition does not explicitly connect these spells to the Shard crisis; the connection is too old, too far removed from the named-doctrine era to survive in any articulated form. But practitioners who have worked deeply with *rootway* report that the networks it accesses feel old in a way other magical infrastructure does not. Pre-historical. As though the paths were not built but were deepened from something that was already there.

### The Solar Court's Answer

On the other side of the desert from the northern forests, the Solar Court's Seal College developed a completely different answer to the same problem. Where the forest practitioners deepened — going further into the living world, committing more vitality to protect what could not be replaced — the Seal College contained. Their reverse-solar geometry was a mathematical framework for enclosing the Shard's effects within a defined boundary, making the anti-light responsible to calculation rather than free to consume whatever it touched.

The containment worked. The Shard did not devour the world. The Pyramid the Solar Court built became both container and proof of the method's validity: external cosmological infrastructure, maintained by institutional practice, holding a cosmic force in check through mathematical precision. This was a genuine achievement — the first time any practitioner tradition had done something that the original vitality exchange could not do, and the first deliberate departure from the original condition. It would eventually produce the cleric tradition's theology of faith as infrastructure, and by extension every tradition that treats power as something channeled from outside the practitioner rather than given from within.

The forest practitioners and the Solar Court did not meet, or if they met, no record of the meeting survived. What each tradition knew of the other, if either knew anything at all, was the indirect knowledge of effects: the forest's deepening might have seemed, to Solar Court observers marking the Shard's influence, as a spontaneous increase in root-system density in northern zones that the erosion was affecting. The forest practitioners' experience of the Shard's eventual cessation — the erosion stopping, the confusion lifting, the accumulated record reasserting itself in the root networks — might have been understood as the living world recovering, the deep networks proving more resilient than the threat, without any awareness that a desert civilization had done something mathematical thousands of leagues away.

### Two Cosmologies

What the encounter established — even as an encounter that was not quite an encounter — were the two primary alternatives for how practitioners can relate to forces larger than themselves.

The Solar Court's answer was containment through external infrastructure: find the mathematical structure that makes the threat responsible to precision. This assumes a cosmic force can be understood well enough to enclose — that it has a defined nature that precision can address, a boundary that calculation can seal.

The forest's answer was survival through biological depth: make the system being threatened too complex and too distributed to be fully consumed. This assumes that the living world's complexity, properly cultivated, is more resilient than any specific threat — that the living archive, propagated through enough organisms and networks and depths, will outlast what attacks it.

Both assumptions were correct for the specific threat each tradition faced. Neither answer would have worked for the other tradition's problem. The forest's deepening could not have contained the Shard — the living world cannot out-complex a force that consumes the complexity of living things. The Solar Court's containment could not have preserved the forest's living archive — mathematical precision works on things with defined edges, and the living archive has no edges except the edges of everything alive.

The druid tradition carries both of these truths as operational knowledge. When the Conclave's crystal matrices entered the northern forests — not a cosmic force but a permanent architectural modification to the substrate the living world ran through — the living archive could not simply deepen further. The Withered Depths order built a quarantine instead. They did something the Solar Court would have recognized: construct an institutional solution to contain a defined threat. It held. And then the last practitioner died, and the containment became a symptom instead of a measure. The Solar Court would have recognized that failure mode too — the institution stopped, so the solution stopped with it.

The forest practitioners would have recognized the deeper problem the Solar Court never faced: that the Conclave's implants were not like the Shard, not a force to contain but a permanent modification to the substrate, already inside the system rather than pressing against it from outside. Neither deepening nor containment addresses that. The tradition's modern practitioners are still working out what does.

## I. The Three Ancient Orders

### The Everkeepers

The Everkeepers were the most formally organized of the three pre-civic druidic traditions and the one that left the most visible physical record. Operating in the ironpine groves of what is now the Great Northern Forest's mid-canopy, the Everkeepers held a cosmological position that was architecturally precise: the ironpines were not merely trees but structural elements, the pillars that held the sky above the earth. Felling an ironpine without planting seven seedlings was not a moral failing or a spiritual transgression — it was a structural violation, equivalent to removing a load-bearing column from a building that would eventually collapse.

This architectural theology shaped everything about Everkeeper practice. Their cultivation rituals were engineering procedures: controlled burns to clear undergrowth that competed with ironpine seedlings, planting-ring placement calculated to maximize seedling survival rates, selective pruning to direct growth toward load-bearing form rather than competitive canopy spread. The **stone planting-rings** that survive throughout the Ironpine Rise — each seven paces across, each oriented to compass points — are the physical record of this work. Modern wardens treat them as protected sites not because of any legal requirement but because they have accumulated sufficient cultural weight that destroying one feels like a specific identifiable act of damage rather than a generalized disturbance.

Everkeeper training was correspondingly rigorous and slow. An apprentice spent the first years learning to read ironpine condition — bark texture, needle color, root-crown indicators — before being taught any ritual practice. The theory was that a practitioner who could not read the tree's current state could not know what the tree needed, and a practitioner who could not know what the tree needed could not perform rituals that were anything other than theatrical. This empirical-first approach distinguished the Everkeepers from more ceremony-oriented traditions and is one of the threads that survived most clearly into the modern druid tradition's emphasis on practical efficacy over spectacular demonstration.

The Ash Winters — three successive volcanic-ash winters approximately two centuries before the current era — killed roughly a third of the Great Northern Forest's large game, disrupted the ironpine's reproductive cycle, and created the conditions that eventually allowed the Ashfang warbands to push deeper into the forest than had previously been possible. The Everkeepers' response to the Ash Winters was principled and demographically catastrophic: they refused to accelerate ironpine growth artificially, on the grounds that stress-accelerated growth produced structurally compromised trees that would weaken the sky-architecture rather than strengthen it. Their adherence to long-term structural integrity at the cost of short-term resilience is remembered with complicated feelings by the Everkeepers' limited successor traditions. The planting-rings continued to appear for two generations after the Ash Winters; then they stopped. The last confirmed Everkeeper ring is dated, by growth-ring analysis of the surviving ironpines, to approximately four human generations before the present era.

Whether any practitioner alive today holds an unbroken lineage from the Everkeepers is contested. The Thornwarden remort path's emphasis on enforcement through physical presence and territorial anchoring shares obvious conceptual DNA with the Everkeepers' sky-architecture maintenance doctrine — but the specific transmission chain, if one exists, is not documented in any source the five cities' archives contain.

### The Forest Druids of the Withered Depths

The druidic order that once tended what is now the Withered Depths left no name behind. This is not an accident of record-keeping — it is consistent with their practice. These druids held that the forest was a living archive, that every tree recorded its history in its growth rings, and that the accumulated memory of an old-growth forest was a form of wisdom that human institutions could never match. A tradition that trusted the forest's own memory had no need to maintain human-institutional records about itself.

What is known comes primarily from two sources: the physical remnants within the Withered Depths themselves (stone circles now partially submerged in the Miasma Bog, interference patterns in Zone 3's thorn geometry where their counter-magic still competes with Conclave crystal resonance), and the oral record maintained by the **Rivennid**, whose mycelial communication network preserves, in a form they do not fully interpret, signals from the period when these druids' rituals were active.

The unnamed order maintained the forest through controlled burning, selective planting, and ritual communion with woodland spirits — methods similar enough to the Everkeepers' that the two traditions must either share a common ancestor or have independently converged on the same practical conclusions. Their stone circles served as gathering places for seasonal rituals that synchronized the forest's growth cycles with celestial patterns, a form of temporal-biological coordination that distinguished them from the Everkeepers' more purely structural approach.

The order's final generation is preserved in the Withered Depths as the **withered druids** — undead remnants of practitioners who died at their containment posts after the Spirebound Conclave's crystal implantations made the blight they had spent their lives containing irreversible. They died performing rituals, and the crystal resonance they had opposed preserved their bodies and bound their spirits to the same endless behavioral loop: perform the ritual, defend the site, perform the ritual. They carry the pulsing blight key — a ritual implement corrupted into a mechanism that responds to the Blightmother's crystal-laced barriers — as a final irony: the tool of containment transformed into an instrument of the thing they were containing against.

The tradition's intersection with the Conclave disaster is the defining event in the five-city region's ecological history. When the Conclave's Prism Thesis adepts implanted crystal matrices in the forest's root systems, the druids' counter-rituals — nature magic in fundamental philosophical conflict with arcane methodology — produced interference patterns rather than containment. Their final creation, the **confusion mists**, was not designed to protect the forest from outsiders but to quarantine the blight's spread. For centuries the quarantine held. When the last druids died, the mists became a symptom rather than a measure, diffusing outward without purpose while the corruption within continued to spread.

The lesson that modern druid practitioners draw from this history is not universally agreed upon. Some emphasize the tradition's heroic dimension — the order maintained its vigil until the last practitioner fell. Others emphasize the structural failure — a tradition that refused to recruit outside a narrow demographic pipeline, that prioritized ideological purity over survival, that died out not through suppression but through demographic arithmetic. Both readings are accurate. The tension between them is one of the unresolved questions that the modern tradition carries.

### The Cairn-Keepers

The third ancient order operated in the high ridges and megalith sites of what is now the Great Northern Forest's Ancestor Deeps. The **Cairn-Keepers** practiced ancestor communion at the standing stones, believing the dead could advise the living through patterns in birdsong, weather shifts, and the behavior of deer herds. Their burial customs placed the honored dead upright in stone niches facing north, toward what they called the Cold Throne — a spiritual seat of final judgment whose resemblance to Kowloon's later covenant stone tradition has been noted and left unexplained by scholars from both cities.

The Cairn-Keepers shared enough methodological DNA with the Withered Depths order — stone circles, ritual calendars, spirit communion — that the two traditions are almost certainly branches of a common ancestor rather than independent developments. The specific nature of the ancestral tradition is not recoverable from available evidence.

What distinguishes the Cairn-Keepers from the other two orders is that they have not entirely dissolved. The stone-antler circles who now guard the megalith sites in the Ancestor Deeps are not the Cairn-Keepers — they are a successor tradition that preserved some of the old knowledge while losing or transforming other elements. But the standing stones remain. The stone niches still hold upright dead, though the most recent interments are several generations old. The ritual permission required to enter the megalith sites is still demanded and granted according to processes that outsiders find opaque but consistent.

The stone-antler circles' relationship with the modern druid tradition is complicated. They do not consider the modern tradition a continuation of the Cairn-Keepers — they consider themselves the continuation. Modern druids who approach the megalith sites seeking access to the old knowledge have been granted that access in a handful of documented cases and denied it in more. The conditions for access, as best as outsiders can reconstruct, involve demonstrating that the petitioner's relationship with the living world is oriented toward maintenance rather than extraction — roughly analogous to the Everkeepers' empirical-first training methodology, but evaluated through ritual rather than examination.

## II. The Transmission Gap and the Reconstruction

None of the three ancient orders survived intact. The Everkeepers' last planting-ring is four human generations old. The Withered Depths order died at its containment posts. The Cairn-Keepers transformed into the stone-antler circles, who consider themselves a continuation but transmit their knowledge only through their own closed community.

The modern druid tradition is therefore not a surviving lineage but a **reconstruction** — an attempt, spanning several generations, to reassemble coherent practice from fragmentary transmissions, inferential archaeology, and direct experimental contact with the living world. This reconstruction history is one of the most consequential facts about the tradition and the one its practitioners are most divided about how to present.

The reconstructors worked from three sources. First, the physical remnants: the planting-rings, the stone circles, the interference patterns in blighted zones where old counter-magic still competes with newer arcane corruption. Reading these remnants required developing a methodology for distinguishing intentional structural choices from incidental effects — a methodology that is itself part of the tradition's intellectual inheritance. Second, the oral record in successor communities: the stone-antler circles, the Mire-Speaker descendants in the Mosswater Bends, the Rivennid's mycelial signal-record that encodes patterns from the pre-collapse era. Third, direct experimental practice: practitioners who went into the forests and learned, through trial and consequence, what relationships with the living world were available and what they cost.

This third source is where the Overgrowth Doctrine emerged.

The reconstructors who worked through direct experimentation discovered something that the ancient orders appear to have understood but never needed to articulate as doctrine, because they learned it from masters who had already internalized it: **the living world's power does not flow to practitioners who hold themselves apart from it**. The arcane traditions — magi, wizards, sorcerers — maintain their power through internal reserves. The cleric tradition draws on cosmological infrastructure maintained at institutional scale. But forest practice, the experimenters found, could not be sustained through either of these models. The forest did not give because you asked. It gave because you were part of the exchange.

The specific form this exchange takes — the Overgrowth mechanic that defines all druid spellwork — is both a practical truth about how forest power functions and a theological statement about the tradition's relationship to that power. Druid spells do not consume mana. They consume the caster's own vitality, paid directly from their hit points. Each casting is a sacrifice of the practitioner's own life energy, offered to the living world in exchange for a response.

The term **Overgrowth** refers to what happens when a practitioner pushes past sustainable sacrifice: when the exchange rate tips from equitable transaction to desperate extraction. A druid who has cast too many spells too quickly enters Overgrowth — a state in which the body's own life force is being consumed by the practice faster than the forest's response can replenish it. In Overgrowth, spells cost increasingly more HP, the body's own growth-processes run ahead of control, and the practitioner's vitality is actively being spent in the act of reaching for more power than their equilibrium with the living world supports.

The theological reading of this mechanic is as contested within the tradition as any other question, but a few formulations have achieved broad enough acceptance to be considered conventional positions.

The **Exchange School** holds that Overgrowth is simply an accurate representation of the underlying physics: you take from the living world, the living world takes from you, and if you take faster than you return, the debt accelerates. There is nothing morally complex about this — it is the same logic as borrowing at interest. The practitioner who manages their relationship well sustains themselves. The one who overextends falls. The mechanic is honest in a way that mana-based systems are not: it shows you exactly what power costs.

The **Covenant School** holds that Overgrowth is not debt but communion taken too far — a form of over-connection that the practitioner enters when they push the relationship with the living world past what either party can sustain without damage. In this reading, Overgrowth is not a failure mode but an expression of successful attunement that has exceeded its safe boundaries: the practitioner is not being punished for overextraction but is instead experiencing the literal consequences of being too deeply entwined with a system larger than themselves. The distinction may seem abstract but produces different training emphases: the Exchange School trains practitioners to manage their expenditures carefully; the Covenant School trains practitioners to know their equilibrium point and work within it.

Both schools agree on the practical reality: a druid in Overgrowth is borrowing against their own life, and the body will settle the debt whether or not the practitioner intends it.

## III. The Overgrowth Doctrine in Practice

Every druid spell is classified by its **growth value** — a measure of how deeply it draws on the practitioner's somatic connection to the living world. Low-growth spells like *sap mend* and *forestsight* draw lightly, leaving the practitioner's equilibrium largely undisturbed. High-growth spells like *upheaval*, *rot*, and *fissure* require the practitioner to commit substantial vitality to achieve their effect, and if cast in sequence, push the practitioner progressively deeper into Overgrowth.

The practical significance of this progression is that druid combat is fundamentally different from arcane or even cleric combat. A wizard who has exhausted their mana cannot cast; they stop. A druid who has exhausted their safe-expenditure range does not stop — they continue casting, but each subsequent spell costs more HP and builds more Overgrowth. The druid can, in principle, cast until they kill themselves, which is a statement the ancient orders would have recognized as both accurate and instructive.

This creates the tradition's characteristic strategic texture: a druid at full vitality is operating in equilibrium, each spell a sustainable exchange. A druid mid-battle is making continuous decisions about how far into Overgrowth to go — how much of their own life to spend to achieve the effect the moment requires. A druid who has pushed deep into Overgrowth is dangerous and fragile simultaneously: their high-end spells are hitting at the top of their power while their body is being consumed by the act of casting them.

The healing spells in the tradition — *sap mend*, *mend wounds*, *verdant mend*, *primal restoration*, and the sacrifice-transfer of *sap transfusion* — exist in a specific relationship to this dynamic. They draw on the same HP pool they restore, which means healing in Overgrowth requires careful calculation: spend life to restore life, at a cost that increases with each prior expenditure. *Sap transfusion* makes the calculus explicit: the practitioner transfers their own HP directly to another, without conversion loss, at the cost of everything they give.

The tradition considers *sap transfusion* the most theologically honest spell in its catalogue. There is nothing metaphorical about it. You give what you give. It either helps or it doesn't.

## IV. The Spell Catalogue: A Philosophical Map

The druid spell catalogue is best understood as a map of the tradition's philosophical commitments rather than a neutral inventory of abilities. Each category of spell reflects a specific understanding of what the practitioner's relationship with the living world requires and permits.

**Offensive earth spells** — *thornlash*, *rockburst*, *briar volley*, *tremor*, *fissure*, and the room-wide *quake* and *upheaval* — express the tradition's understanding that the earth itself is a participant in conflict, not merely a backdrop for it. A thornlash is not a practitioner throwing thorns at a target; it is the practitioner channeling a directive into root systems that respond by projecting defensive growth outward. The distinction matters because it shapes how Overgrowth is experienced: the practitioner is not generating force from internal reserves but asking the earth to act, at the cost of their own vitality as the medium of that asking. When the earth complies, the expenditure was justified. When it doesn't — a saved spell doing reduced damage — the practitioner still pays, which is an honest accounting of what it means to ask something of a system that retains its own agency.

**Offensive poison and rot spells** — *nettlesting*, *wither*, *blight touch*, *creeping rot*, *rot*, and the room-scale *fungal eruption* — reflect the tradition's engagement with decay as a natural process rather than a transgression. Where Necromancer practice treats decay as a resource extracted from unwilling systems, druid practice treats decay as a legitimate ecological mechanism that the practitioner can direct. The forest kills. It always has. The practitioner who channels *rot* is not introducing a foreign force; they are directing an existing process. The ethical weight of this — choosing to accelerate decay in a living thing — is not absent from the tradition's self-examination, but it is contextualized differently than it would be in a tradition that understood itself as standing outside natural processes.

**Entanglement and restraint spells** — *strangling vines*, *tangling roots*, *entangling growth*, *roothold* — are the tradition's most distinctively non-arcane offensive tools. They do not deal damage as their primary function; they restrict movement and reduce effectiveness through the most direct possible application of forest logic: growth is relentless, patient, and does not negotiate. A target enmeshed in *strangling vines* is experiencing something that will continue regardless of what they prefer. The practitioner's role is simply to initiate what the living world will complete.

**Defensive and buff spells** — *barkskin*, *thornhide*, *ancient bark*, *ironpine vigor*, *gift of the forest*, *nature's ward* — reflect the Everkeepers' structural inheritance most directly. The practitioner is not armoring themselves with magical constructs but incorporating the forest's own defensive adaptations into their body. *Barkskin* is not an illusion of bark; it is the actual process of bark-like structural density developing in the body's surface tissues. *Ironpine vigor* channels the ironpine's specific growth characteristic — the particular density that makes ironpine the tree the Everkeepers considered a sky-pillar — into the practitioner's own physical reserves. The borrowing is temporary, but while it holds, the practitioner is genuinely carrying the ironpine's contribution.

**Healing spells** are discussed in the Overgrowth section above. They represent the tradition's most direct statement of what it means to work with life rather than on it.

**Utility spells** — *forestsight*, *sense life*, *greensight*, *know terrain*, *passage*, *rootway*, *call of the wild*, *living bridge* — are the tradition's sensory and mobility tools, and they reveal an important aspect of the tradition's epistemology: druid perception is ecological perception. *Forestsight* grants awareness of hidden things because the forest is already tracking them; the practitioner is tapping into an existing intelligence network rather than creating new surveillance capacity. *Sense life* perceives invisible entities because the forest's sensitivity to living presence operates on different registers than human vision. *Know terrain* is what happens when a practitioner's attunement is deep enough to read their environment the way the environment reads itself.

*Rootway* and *living bridge* are the most theoretically interesting utility spells. Both are transportation magic — analogous to teleport and portal — but their mechanism in the tradition's understanding is specifically arboreal: the practitioner is moving through the mycelial and root networks that connect trees across large distances rather than through abstract magical space. Whether this is accurate or a metaphor that the tradition finds productive, the spells work in ways that practitioners in other traditions find difficult to replicate or counter.

## V. The Remort Paths and the Adept Designation

The druid lineage's first branching, available to practitioners who have completed their foundational training, offers two specialized remort paths. The adept designation integrates both.

### Thornwarden

The **Thornwarden** is the tradition's martial-enforcement specialization, developing the foundational druid's capacities toward territorial boundary-declaration and physical presence as the living world's edge. The path's name and doctrine trace directly to the Everkeepers' thorn-boundary practice — the bramble-walls that declared planting-rings under cultivation. Where a foundational druid casts from range, the Thornwarden becomes the thorn-wall in mobile form: the point at which the forest's patience runs out and something with thorns is standing where you hoped to pass.

The Thornwarden's enhanced damage output to earth spells reflects not aggression but structural heritage: strikes that land with the weight of things that have been growing toward this moment for a long time. The path also develops the combat applications of substrate piercing and the full defensive ecology of barkskin, thornhide, and ancient bark.

For full Thornwarden lore — the Everkeeper inheritance, boundary doctrine, civic institution relationships, and the Dunmar School cooperation — see `thornwarden_lore.md`.

### Wildspeaker

The **Wildspeaker** is the tradition's ecological-communion specialization, developing the foundational druid's capacities toward deep attunement with the living world's informational systems, biological-reading healing, and the full deployment of the sensory and network-mobility spell arc. The path's primary historical ancestry is the unnamed Withered Depths order whose ecological knowledge is the tradition's richest — and whose demographic failure is the path's named cautionary example for immersion failure.

What Wildspeakers develop is not linguistic communication with the forest but the capacity to receive the living world's continuous broadcast of its own state at actionable resolution. Their healing is more precise than the foundational druid's because it reads what damage actually is rather than what it looks like. Their mobility through the root and mycelial network is, in the tradition's understanding, not teleportation but traversal of an existing living circulatory system.

For full Wildspeaker lore — the Withered Depths lineage, ecological signal reading, the healing doctrine, immersion failure, and the Parallel Record Compact relationship — see `wildspeaker_lore.md`.

### Hierophant

The **Hierophant** designation is available to practitioners who have completed both remort paths and demonstrated genuine integration — not the ability to do both Thornwarden and Wildspeaker things, but the elimination of the transition between them. The practitioner who is truly deep-rooted holds boundary and receives signal simultaneously. The Cairn-Keepers' stone-antler successors use that term — *deep-rooted* — for the quality they evaluate in megalith-site petitioners; Hierophants are the druid tradition's practitioners most reliably described this way.

Hierophants are the tradition's rarest outcome and its most complete answer to the Living Archive Problem: the practitioners most likely to be capable of the maintenance functions the ancient orders served, and most conspicuously absent from the current discussions about whether those functions need to be performed again.

For full Hierophant lore — what integration actually means, the CON ceiling, the succession problem, and the relationship to the ancient orders' maintenance functions — see `hierophant_lore.md`.

## VI. Druids Within the Five Cities

The druid tradition's relationship with civic institutions is structurally similar to its relationship with the ancient orders' successor communities: complicated, not institutionalized, and defined more by what each party chooses not to do to the other than by any positive framework of cooperation.

No city in the five-city network has a formal druid registry. The Violet Compact's registry framework, which formalized every other practicing tradition that wanted civic standing, did not produce a druid registry — not because the Compact excluded druids but because no sufficient population of practicing druids existed at the time the registry framework was being established, and the fragmentary reconstruction effort was not yet producing practitioners in numbers that made civic registration a pressing question on either side.

This means that modern druids exist in a legally ambiguous position relative to registry law. They are not registered, and they are not banned. Civic officials who encounter them typically categorize them under general "unregistered practitioner" provisions, which require registration if they want to practice openly in commercial or civic contexts, and are typically enforced with the same inconsistency that characterizes all Violet Compact-era registration requirements.

### Midgaard

Midgaard's relationship with the druid tradition is shaped primarily by the Great Northern Forest and the Eccentric Woodland — the two major forest environments proximate to the city. Druids who operate in either area are subject to informal negotiation rather than formal regulation: road wardens in the northern forest extend professional courtesy to Thornwarden practitioners in ways that the registry office is not formally aware of, and the Eccentric Woodland's status as effectively unregulated civic territory means practitioners there are subject to no meaningful oversight regardless of tradition.

Within the city itself, druid practitioners are rare enough to be notable. Midgaard's arcanist registry — which covers arcane practice broadly — has a standing position that nature magic practiced outside the registry framework is "pending categorization," which functionally means it is tolerated until someone complains about it specifically. This has been the registry's position for approximately forty years, and the pending categorization has not been resolved.

The **Continuity faction** within Midgaard's politics is uninterested in formalizing the druid tradition. Formalization would require examining the tradition's relationship to the institutional history the Continuity faction prefers undisturbed — specifically, the chain of events in which Midgaard's sealed warrants contributed to the Conclave's forest research that the druid orders spent their final generations trying to contain. A formal druid registry would create pressure to acknowledge what the druids were containing and why it needed containment.

The **Reckoning faction** has been the more natural institutional ally for druids who want civic recognition. Some Reckoning-aligned scholars have publicly argued that the druid tradition represents the most direct institutional acknowledgment of Midgaard's historical debts to the northern forests — that formalizing the tradition would require the city to confront what was done to the environments these practitioners are the inheritors of. This argument has not been persuasive to the Reckoning faction's more practically-minded members, who note that the druid tradition's non-registration is currently working in the tradition's favor and that formalization carries its own risks.

### Kowloon

Kowloon's charter covenant structure might seem more hospitable to the druid tradition than Midgaard's registry framework — covenants are relationships, and the druid tradition is fundamentally relational — but in practice, Kowloon's civic culture has been more skeptical of the tradition than Midgaard's inattention. Kowloon's founding mythology emphasizes collective human response to natural crisis (the Ashfall Monsoon, the Neon Covenant's engineering and logistics mobilization) rather than harmony with natural systems. The tradition that asks practitioners to put themselves in biological communion with the forest is not easily mapped onto a civic culture that understood its founding moment as humans successfully dominating the conditions that had previously dominated them.

Druid practitioners in Kowloon tend to work in contexts that Kowloon's civic framework can categorize as technical rather than magical — herbalists with unusual botanical knowledge, environmental assessors with accurate predictions about seasonal conditions, wilderness guides whose success rates are explained by experience rather than attunement. Whether this is camouflage or simply an accurate description of what druid practice looks like at the non-spectacular end of its range is a question the tradition's Kowloon practitioners do not typically engage with publicly.

The forest north of the city — which Kowloon's northern crown patrols manage — contains old stone-antler circle territory that Kowloon's Jade Magistracy maps include but declines to adjudicate. The Magistracy's position is that the megalith sites predate any charter authority the city holds and therefore are not subject to Kowloon's covenant framework. This position is legally honest and practically convenient: it avoids the question of what Kowloon's relationship to those sites would be if the Magistracy attempted to assert jurisdiction and was told by the stone-antler circles exactly what the relationship would be.

### Kiess

Kiess is, counterintuitively, the most legally hospitable city for druid practice in the five-city network. The same anti-sealed-warrant charter provisions that protect access to Solar Court scholarship records also prevent the city from enforcing pre-existing registry designations imported from Midgaard or Kowloon. A druid who enters Kiess and begins practicing openly is subject to Kiess's own charter framework — which, given the city's foundational emphasis on individual operational flexibility and institutional skepticism, is substantially more permissive than either of the larger cities' frameworks.

Kiess's proximity to the Withered Depths and the Forest of Confusion also creates genuine civic demand for druid practitioners. Scouts who operate in the Forest of Confusion's confusion-mist zones benefit from having practitioners who can navigate using forest-intelligence inputs rather than conventional wayfinding. The city's quartermaster supply trains that cycle to forest-edge staging trails have found that Wildspeaker practitioners can reduce the incidence of route confusion in the southern Confusion Forest verge — not because they are immune to the mists, but because they are sensitive to the ecological signals that indicate when mist density is increasing.

Kiess is also where the most serious scholarly work on the Withered Depths druids' records is being done. The Syndic Council Annex holds documents that connect to the druidic quarantine history in ways the Council declines to discuss, but independent scholars working within Kiess's open-information charter have been assembling the connection between the old druids' confusion-mist creation and the Withered Depths' current advance. Some of these scholars are themselves druid practitioners; others are not. The collaboration is typical of Kiess's intellectual culture: unchartered, productive, and producing findings that other cities' institutions find inconvenient.

### Rakuen and Mafdet

Rakuen's druid practitioners work primarily in the city's disaster-response capacity, where the tradition's healing spells and environmental-assessment capabilities have immediate practical value. A Rakuen Wildspeaker who can assess whether a collapsed structure's surrounding soil is stable, or monitor whether a blocked waterway is building pressure toward a secondary failure, is providing a specific service that the city's chronic-emergency posture creates constant demand for. Rakuen druids are the most practically tested of all the tradition's city practitioners, and they are correspondingly less concerned with theoretical questions about the tradition's history and more concerned with whether the spells work at the moment they need to.

Mafdet's relationship with the druid tradition is minimal. The city's dominant spiritual framework — the Mafdet guardian tradition, the Shrine of the First Claw's protective-executioner theology — has no natural overlap with the living-world-communion approach, and Mafdet's arid geography does not provide the ecological substrate that druid practice most naturally operates within. Druid practitioners who pass through Mafdet typically do so as travelers rather than residents, and the city's institutions treat them with the same generalized indifference they extend to most non-Mafdet spiritual traditions.

## VII. The Living Archive Problem

The most pressing unresolved question in the modern druid tradition is also the oldest: what is the tradition actually trying to preserve, and is it preserving it?

The ancient orders understood themselves as maintenance institutions. The Everkeepers maintained the sky-architecture. The Withered Depths druids maintained the quarantine and the forest's internal memory. The Cairn-Keepers' successors maintain the megalith sites and the ancestor-communion practice. All three understood their work as something that would stop if they stopped — that required ongoing human attention because the things they were maintaining would not sustain themselves without it.

The modern reconstruction faces the same logic with less certainty about what is actually requiring maintenance. The Withered Depths' spread is the most concrete evidence that the old maintenance function has lapsed: the confusion-mist quarantine the last druids created is now a symptom rather than a containment measure, and the Depths are advancing along routes that match the Conclave's old survey corridors in ways that suggest directed accumulation rather than simple ecological spread. Whether druid practice can address this, and if so how, is a question the tradition has not answered.

The **Parallel Record Compact** — the coalition of Rivennid, Khephari, Serathi, Deltari, and Kethari observers whose coordinated analysis has triangulated a specific interior location as the Depths' apparent accumulation target — has not formally approached the druid tradition for collaboration. The Compact's analysis has been circulated in Kiess scholarly circles where druid practitioners have had access to it. Whether the tradition has the institutional capacity to mount a response, or whether the reconstruction is still too fragmented to coordinate at that scale, is actively being debated.

The Everkeepers' planting-rings present a different version of the same problem. The rings are maintained by road wardens as protected sites, but no one is maintaining the practice they represent. The ironpines within them continue to grow, and by growth-ring analysis, they are growing well — the structural engineering the Everkeepers did in establishing the sites was sound enough to be self-sustaining for generations. But the rings are no longer being added to. The tradition that produced them is not adding new rings or tending the existing ones through the same attentive practice that created them. The rings are surviving, but the relationship that produced them is not.

This is what the tradition calls the **Living Archive Problem**: the forest holds the record of what the druid orders did, and the record is more persistent than the orders themselves, but the record is not the same as the practice. A practitioner who can read the planting-rings is reading the Everkeepers' conclusions, not following their method. A practitioner who can decode the Rivennid's mycelial signals is accessing the Withered Depths druids' output, not performing their rituals. The physical world preserves the trace of maintenance. It does not preserve the capacity for maintenance.

The tradition's response to this problem is not resolved. The Exchange School holds that the reconstruction's experimental approach — practitioners going into the forests and learning directly from the living world what relationships are available — is the appropriate method: if the forest holds the record, the forest can teach what the record means. The Covenant School holds that without the thread of transmission from masters who knew the full practice, the reconstruction is necessarily incomplete in ways that cannot be corrected by experimental practice alone. Both positions are held by serious practitioners. Neither has prevailed.

The stone-antler circles, who are not part of the reconstruction project but are the living continuation of one of the ancient orders' successors, maintain a posture of watchful patience toward the druid tradition's reconstruction effort. They have granted access to megalith sites in enough cases to make clear the access is not impossible. They have declined it in enough cases to make clear they are evaluating something specific. What they are waiting for the tradition to demonstrate, if they are waiting for anything, is not something they have communicated in terms that the reconstruction's Exchange School practitioners find interpretable.

The Covenant School practitioners think they know what it is, but they do not say so in terms the Exchange School would accept.
