# Lore: The Khenari

## Core Identity

The **Khenari** are jackal-headed humanoids whose civilization emerged from the mortuary-legal culture of Akh'enet. They are psychopomps, balance-keepers, and death-administrators — a people who do not merely worship at the boundary between the living and the dead but who have built their entire society around managing that threshold as a civic institution.

Where humans treat death as interruption, the Khenari treat it as **transaction**. Where humans mourn, the Khenari **record**. This is not cruelty but precision: in a world where unrecorded deaths collapse inheritance chains, destabilize water-rights, and unravel road-law, the Khenari's meticulous death-ledgers are the connective tissue of civilization.

---

## Origin

Khenari trace their lineage to the **Jackal Balance Court** of Akh'enet — the institution that fused debt adjudication with funerary sentence rites during the Ledger Kingdom Era. Oral tradition claims they were not originally a separate people but became one through generations of immersion in death-ritual: that the constant administration of passage between life and death gradually refracted their bloodlines into something distinct, their features sharpening into the long-nosed, amber-eyed aspect of the desert jackal.

Scholars dispute whether the transformation was literal or metaphorical. The Khenari themselves consider the question meaningless. "We carry the record," is their saying. "What we are is what we have always done."

Their connection to the city of **Akh'enet** is deep even now that the city lies in ruin. The Bellwind Collapse scattered the major court families outward along caravan routes, and Khenari communities can now be found wherever death-records and legal continuity are valued: in mortuary districts of larger cities, as caravan record-keepers, and as traveling magistrates who adjudicate disputes over inheritance and road-debt in remote settlements.

---

## Physical Description

Khenari stand slightly shorter than average humans, with lean, efficient frames built for long desert travel rather than raw combat. Their most distinctive features are their elongated, tapered muzzles — somewhere between jackal and wolf in profile — and their tall, triangular ears that rotate independently to track sound. Their eyes are amber to deep gold, with slit pupils that widen dramatically in low light, giving them excellent vision in the perpetual desert night.

Their fur ranges from sandy tawny through warm brown to deep charcoal, often with lighter patches at the throat and chest. Many Khenari carry elaborate ritual scarification along the outer ears and muzzle, patterns that in traditional communities encode lineage, professional role, and the number of death-ledgers one has formally closed.

They have long jackal tails that serve as involuntary emotional indicators — a source of significant social embarrassment when their careful professional demeanor fails, since the tail communicates what the face does not.

## Culture and Society

### The Ledger as Sacred Object

In Khenari culture, the death-ledger is not merely a bureaucratic document but a sacred object. To close a ledger — to record the final accounting of a life's debts, contracts, and obligations — is a religious act. Khenari who have closed a hundred ledgers are addressed with the same honorific as priests. Those who falsify records are not merely criminals but heretics.

This reverence extends to memory itself. The Khenari maintain oral traditions of extraordinary length and precision: genealogies, debt-lineages, legal precedent chains. A trained Khenari mnemonist can recite the death-ledger entries of three generations of a trading family from memory, including marginal notes about contested items.

### The Doctrine of Ninth Descent

Khenari spiritual life is organized around **the Ninth Descent** — the belief that lawful passage from life to death requires nine distinct administrative stages, each requiring a different type of witness and a different form of record. The doctrine originated in the Moon Sovereign's clerical influence over Akh'enet but was formalized and systematized by Khenari jurist-priests over generations.

The nine descents are:
1. **Declaration** — the dying person's final testament
2. **Witness** — confirmation by a qualified observer
3. **Inventory** — physical accounting of possessions
4. **Debt resolution** — settlement of outstanding obligations
5. **Lineage notation** — formal record of surviving heirs
6. **Route closure** — discharge of any road-law obligations
7. **Name sealing** — formal entry in the death-ledger
8. **Silence passage** — a period of ritual silence in which the name is held before being fully released
9. **Verdict** — the final determination of whether the passage was lawful and complete

A death that skips any of the nine descents is considered **unlawful passage** — a source of ongoing spiritual instability that can affect the dead person's family for generations.

### The Shadow and the Verdict

Khenari religion positions shadow not as evil but as **necessary condition for judgment**. The same darkness that conceals wrongdoing also conceals the sacred process of adjudication — the verdict should be reached in shadow so that neither party can influence the judge through spectacle or social pressure. The great silence-courts of Akh'enet, where testimony was "clarified" through acoustic deprivation and chant intervals, were Khenari inventions: not torture chambers but mechanisms for reaching verdicts untainted by performance.

This relationship with shadow makes Khenari naturally gifted in dark environments and resistant to mental intrusion — centuries of training in the silence-courts have produced a people whose minds are organized against noise, distraction, and psychic pressure.

Their simultaneous command of holy and shadow magic reflects this duality: death belongs to both. The divine sanctions the passage; the darkness enables the honest verdict.

## Racial Traits

**Stat Profile:** STR 12 | INT 18 | WIS 22 | DEX 16 | CON 16

Khenari are not warriors. They are judges, scribes, and psychopomps — people whose physical frailty is offset by extraordinary perceptive capacity and accumulated wisdom. Their high Wisdom reflects generations of legal and theological training; their moderate Intelligence reflects a people who are deeply learned but whose knowledge is specialized rather than broadly inventive.

**Elemental Affinities:**
- **Strong in:** Shadow, Holy — Khenari spellwork excels in death-magic, darkness-command, and divine verdict
- **Weak in:** Physical, Fire — direct violence and consuming flame sit outside their tradition
- **Resistant to:** Shadow, Mental — shadow cannot harm shadow; the silence-court training makes Khenari minds difficult to penetrate
- **Susceptible to:** Fire, Physical — direct blows and burning disrupt a people whose survival strategy has always been avoidance, record-keeping, and legal maneuvering rather than physical endurance

**Racial Skills:** sneak, spell critical damage, spell critical

The **sneak** ability reflects the Khenari tradition of moving through contested spaces undetected — a skill developed over generations of operating in ruins, tombs, and hostile court environments where a careless footstep could end a negotiation or a life.

**Spell critical damage** and **spell critical** reflect the extraordinary precision of Khenari spellcasting: where others cast widely, Khenari cast with exactitude, delivering spiritual force to the precise point where it will do the most damage.

**Racial Modifiers:** Darkness (natural dark-sight, shadow-culture), Tail

**Wear Slots:** Head, Face (not Horns or Beak), Hands (not Claws), Tail

## Relationship to the World

### Akh'enet

The ruined city remains the spiritual center of Khenari identity even for those who have never seen it. Pilgrimage to Akh'enet's surviving mortuary terraces is a rite of passage for Khenari jurist-priests. The factional conflicts between the Reed Ledger Wardens, the Moon Sepulchrists, and the Salt Jackal Reclaimers are Khenari family politics writ large — most Khenari communities trace lineage to one faction or another and carry the ancestral arguments with them.

### The Great Pyramid

The Pyramid's judicial function — as a machine that weighs souls and issues verdicts — resonates deeply with Khenari theology. Many Khenari believe the Pyramid's sentence-binding mechanisms were derived from or parallel to the Jackal Balance Court's own practices. Whether the Court borrowed from the Pyramid or whether both drew from an older common source is a theological debate that has lasted centuries.

### Midgaard and Kowloon

Khenari communities in both cities cluster in mortuary and legal districts, often serving as notaries, estate administrators, or death-record keepers. They are valued for their meticulous accuracy but regarded with some wariness — Khenari notaries are famously incorruptible, which makes them useful and inconvenient in equal measure.

## Narrative Tone

Khenari NPCs should feel like bureaucrats who happen to have access to divine power, or priests who happen to express their faith through paperwork. They are not cold — they feel grief and joy as strongly as anyone — but they process emotion through ritual and record rather than expression. A Khenari comforting a widow is likely to help her complete the death-ledger before offering condolences; not because they lack empathy but because they believe the paperwork is itself a form of care.

They are suspicious of improvisation and distrust those who move through the world without documentation. An undocumented stranger in a Khenari neighborhood is not a mystery but a problem — a gap in the record that needs to be filled.
