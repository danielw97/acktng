# Lore: The Necromancer Tradition

## Overview

The Necromancer tradition is older than the Psionicist academies that now claim jurisdiction over the mind arts. Before the academies there were the **Entropy Scholars** — independent practitioners who studied decay and dissolution because they believed that understanding endings was essential to understanding existence. Over centuries their research extended into practical application: sustaining the departed past their natural terminus, drawing on residual spiritual energy as a power source, weaponizing the same processes that unravel living systems. The academies branded them heretics. The Entropy Scholars noted that death itself cannot be outlawed by institutional consensus. Psionicists who return to study entropy rather than enhancement carry on that ancient, unsanctioned tradition.

---

## I. The Entropy Scholars

The **Entropy Scholars** emerged independently of both the Solar Court's theological framework and the later academic magical traditions — which is to say, they operated without the institutional context that might have either integrated their practice into a legitimate framework or directly suppressed it. They were practitioners who studied what happened at the end of things: the specific processes by which living systems dissolved, what residual properties survived dissolution, and what those residual properties could tell them about how living systems had functioned.

The foundational methodology of the Entropy Scholars was **comparative dissolution analysis** — studying the decay of living systems in sufficient detail to understand which structural properties persisted after apparent dissolution and which were genuinely lost. This required working with death in ways that most institutional frameworks considered either religiously transgressive (the Solar Court's Embalmer Houses held death rites as proprietary sacred function) or legally problematic (the charter road system's corpse-handling regulations were extensive and specific).

Operating outside these frameworks, Entropy Scholars developed their practice in geographic and institutional margins: abandoned cistern complexes where unclaimed bodies accumulated after Ninth Meridian chaos, battlefield sites where the scale of death exceeded any institution's capacity for proper processing, areas adjacent to the Great Pyramid where the Black Sun Shard's memory-consuming properties created anomalous death-and-decay phenomena that conventional theology could not explain.

The Black Sun Shard connection is significant and rarely acknowledged in the tradition's formal documentation. The Shard's documented effects — the dead rising with incorrect memories, households forgetting their bloodlines, the dead failing to fully depart — are, from an Entropy Scholar's analytical perspective, documentation of the boundary between life and death being insufficiently robust. The Shard doesn't just cause deaths; it reveals structural properties of the life-death boundary that are normally invisible. The Entropy Scholars who worked in Shard-adjacent areas had access to phenomena that practitioners working in normal conditions never observed.

## II. The Heresy Designation

The **Psionicist academies'** designation of Entropy Scholar practice as heretical is historically interesting because the academies and the Entropy Scholars did not obviously occupy competing domains. The academies studied mind and mental phenomena; the Scholars studied entropy and death phenomena. The overlap was not immediately obvious.

The conflict developed when the academies, consolidating their institutional authority over a specific category of practice they called "the mind arts," found that Entropy Scholar research had been producing results in adjacent domains — specifically, the study of what mental residue survived death and how it could be accessed. The academies' jurisdiction claim was sufficiently broad to include this territory; the Entropy Scholars had been working there for generations before the academies existed; neither the Scholars nor the academies had obvious legal priority.

The "heresy" designation was the academies' response to the jurisdictional conflict. By declaring Entropy Scholar practice heretical, the academies positioned themselves as not merely competing with the Scholars but as having legitimate institutional authority to declare the Scholars' practice illegitimate. This framing worked in institutional contexts where the academies had leverage — credentialing requirements, research funding access, sanctuary status — and failed in contexts where the academies had no leverage, which included anywhere outside the academic city network and anyone who didn't need academy credentials to do their work.

The Entropy Scholars' response — that death cannot be outlawed by institutional consensus — was correct in the specific sense that entropy is a feature of physical reality that will continue operating regardless of academic institutional positions. The more practically significant response was the Scholars' development of an independent transmission structure (independent of academy oversight) that has allowed the tradition to persist through multiple eras of institutional pressure.

## III. Practical Necromancy: Sustaining, Drawing, Weaponizing

The three practical applications that define modern Necromancer practice each developed from distinct threads of Entropy Scholar research:

**Sustaining the departed past their natural terminus** is the application that produces the most public controversy and the most institutionally visible practice. The Solar Court's Embalmer Houses had performed a version of this function — preparing bodies in ways that preserved their form past the point of normal decay — but the Embalmer function was service to the deceased's continuity, not command of it. Entropy Scholar practice of sustaining departed entities as operationally directed forces is categorically distinct: it treats the residual properties that survive dissolution as resources that the practitioner controls rather than as the deceased's own persistent presence.

The theological frameworks of the five cities' religious institutions — clerics, priests, and Paladin-affiliated orders — are uniformly hostile to this application. The Solar Court's theology held that the dead's residual presence was under the Jackal Tribunal's jurisdiction; the departed should be processed through the appropriate channels, not redirected by practitioners who lack the Tribunal's authority. Modern cleric doctrine holds that unauthorized sustaining of departed entities constitutes interference with the cosmic accounting function that keeps the Black Sun Shard's memory-consuming effects from propagating — that the departed's proper processing is part of the machinery that maintains the world's metaphysical stability.

Necromancers whose compact with this critique is honest tend to find it neither conclusively refuted nor wholly convincing. The cleric tradition's argument depends on the Solar Court's institutional authority remaining legitimate — which is a complicated claim in a world where the Solar Court has been inaccessible for centuries. The Entropy Scholars' counter-argument is that claiming institutional authority over phenomena that your institution can no longer actually monitor is either wishful thinking or deliberate power-maintenance.

**Drawing on residual spiritual energy as a power source** is less theologically controversial than sustaining and more technically sophisticated. The residual energy that Entropy Scholars identified as persisting through dissolution is not static — it dissipates over time, at rates that vary depending on the intensity of the original living system and the conditions of the dissolution. Practitioners who can access this energy before it dissipates have access to a power source that requires no active maintenance, no ongoing consent relationship, and no extraction from living systems — it is, in the Entropy Scholars' analysis, simply using something that would otherwise be lost.

The counter-argument — that residual spiritual energy belongs to the departed entity and using it constitutes a form of violation — is one the Entropy Scholars and modern Necromancers engage with by noting that the departed entity has, by definition, ceased to have active interests in the philosophical sense that generates moral claims. The debate has not been resolved to anyone's satisfaction.

**Weaponizing decay processes** is the most technically direct of the three applications and the one that requires the least theological engagement to perform. The specific processes by which living systems dissolve — oxidative damage, cellular breakdown, immune system dysfunction — are real biological phenomena that can be accelerated through specific applications of the Entropy Scholars' techniques. A practitioner who understands the mechanisms of decay at a technical level can apply those mechanisms to accelerate specific aspects of an opponent's physical functioning toward their terminal states.

The application is effective and disturbing to observe, which is why Necromancers' reputation in popular culture emphasizes this application over the more sophisticated (and arguably more interesting) theoretical aspects of the tradition.

## IV. The Death-Administrative Lineage

The Entropy Scholars' emergence in "geographic and institutional margins" is not a random geographical accident — the margins they occupied were specifically the spaces adjacent to existing death-administrative institutions that the Entropy Scholars were working beyond those institutions' authority. Three such institutional contexts are particularly significant for understanding what the modern Necromancer tradition is actually an extension of, rather than a departure from:

**Akh'enet's mortuary-port structure**: Akh'enet institutionalized death as a municipal transfer process over multiple eras of the desert civilization's development. Contracts, debt obligations, and civic inheritance were designed to survive the person; tomb architecture included filing niches, tally grooves, and adjudication benches alongside religious iconography. The **Jackal Balance Court** — one of Akh'enet's defining institutions — fused debt adjudication with funerary sentence rites to the point where legal process and death administration used the same vocabulary. A judgment issued from the Court's silence chambers carried authority over both the debtor's living estate and the disposition of the debtor's remains.

The Entropy Scholars who worked in and around Akh'enet were not working against this institutional logic. They were extending it past the point where Akh'enet's institutions had stopped. Where the Jackal Balance Court processed the dead through institutional channels — determining what obligations survived, what records were transferred, what property was redistributed — the Entropy Scholars asked what remained after the processing was complete. The question was not theologically transgressive by Akh'enet's institutional standards; it was simply further than Akh'enet's institutions had authorization to go.

The tradition's **condense soul** and **soul net** techniques — which capture and concentrate residual spiritual energy before it dissipates — are the most direct technical inheritances of this Akh'enet methodological lineage. The techniques treat residual spiritual energy exactly as Akh'enet's courts treated residual legal obligations: as something that survives the person's death and can be formally captured and directed rather than allowed to dissipate unmanaged.

**The Solar Court's Embalmer Houses**: One of the Solar Court's five institutional offices, the Embalmer Houses performed the physical preparation of the dead for the cosmological transitions the Solar Court's theology required. Their methods were not merely preservationist — they were based on a working theory of what the body's relationship to spiritual residue required in physical terms, and their techniques for managing that relationship without disturbing the Black Sun Shard's containment represented a sophisticated institutional response to real cosmological dangers.

The Entropy Scholars' distinction from the Embalmer Houses was not primarily methodological but jurisdictional: the Embalmer Houses operated in service of the deceased's institutional continuity, facilitating transitions that the Solar Court's cosmology specified. The Entropy Scholars operated in the analytical mode — studying what those transitions actually involved, rather than facilitating them through traditional channels. When the Embalmer Houses prepared a body, they were maintaining the machinery of cosmic order. When the Entropy Scholars observed the same process from outside the institution, they were learning how the machinery worked.

Modern Necromancer practice's **revenant** technique — the sustained direction of a departed entity as an operational force — represents the most direct divergence from the Embalmer Houses' approach. Where the Embalmer Houses' work facilitated the departed entity's transition to whatever the Solar Court's theology specified next, the revenant technique retains the entity in active directed service. The Embalmer Houses would have considered this a violation of the departure's natural process; the Entropy Scholars' descendants note that the Embalmer Houses no longer exist and the Solar Court's institutional authority to specify what constitutes a natural departure process is no longer operational.

**The Khenari death-ritual specialists**: The Khenari people had developed death-ritual administrative expertise over centuries as part of the broader institutional apparatus surrounding the Solar Court and the desert pyramid civilization. Their methods for managing the transition between living and deceased status — the documentation procedures, physical preparation techniques, and specific protocols that prevented the Black Sun Shard's memory-consuming effects from propagating inappropriately through death transitions — constituted a body of practical knowledge that operated independently of the Embalmer Houses' more theologically oriented practice.

Entropy Scholars who worked in areas where Khenari practitioners operated found that the Khenari's death-administrative techniques addressed some of the same practical problems from a different direction. The Khenari's knowledge of how to manage death transitions without triggering adverse Black Sun Shard interactions was, from the Entropy Scholars' analytical perspective, knowledge about the specific mechanisms through which the life-death boundary could be managed rather than simply crossed.

The debt that the Entropy Scholar tradition — and by extension the modern Necromancer tradition — owes to Khenari practical knowledge is documented in records that the Khenari themselves maintained and that are preserved in Kiess's anti-sealed-warrant archives under the classification "disputed institutional precedence claims." The documentation records specific instances where Entropy Scholars consulted Khenari practitioners and incorporated their techniques without formal acknowledgment. Whether the modern Necromancer tradition characterizes this as historical appropriation or as the normal transmission of knowledge across practitioner communities without institutional framework depends substantially on which position the specific practitioner finds more comfortable.

What is not in dispute is the historical scope of death-management knowledge that predates the Entropy Scholars' own research: Akh'enet's administrative mortuary tradition, the Solar Court's Embalmer Houses, and the Khenari's independent expertise constitute a centuries-deep lineage of institutionalized death knowledge that the Entropy Scholars worked in the margins of, and that modern Necromancer practice is the continuation of — through different channels, without the institutional authorization, but in genuine methodological continuity.

## V. The Kinetimancer Synthesis

The **Kinetimancer** adept path represents the synthesis of Necromancer entropy practice and **Egomancer** will-imposition practice — a combination that the tradition's founding documentation, the **Kinetic Formulists'** manuscripts, described as demonstrating that both traditions were working with **momentum**.

Entropy practice redirects the momentum of decay: rather than allowing dissolution to proceed at its natural rate in its natural direction, the practitioner redirects it toward a specific target at an accelerated rate. Will-imposition practice redirects the momentum of intent: rather than allowing a target's cognitive processes to proceed according to their own assessment, the practitioner imposes alternative assessments that redirect the target's effective intent. The Kinetic Formulists' insight was that these are structural analogues — the underlying operation in both cases is **redirection of momentum**, applied to different domains.

The synthesis does not merely combine two traditions; it creates a new application that neither tradition alone could produce: the redirection of momentum at the boundary between physical and cognitive domains, where entropy-based dissolution of cognitive function and will-based imposition of alternative cognitive states interact in ways that produce effects beyond what either technique achieves in its domain alone.
