# Lore: The Paladin Tradition

## Overview

Paladins are oathbound vanguards — a tradition born from the specific crisis of the **Sundering Wars**, when mortal armies desecrated temples and the gods were said to have wept. The clerics who watched this happen and resolved never to leave their sanctuaries defenseless again became the first Paladins: practitioners who forsook pure ministry without abandoning faith, took up arms without becoming soldiers, and swore binding oaths to the light itself rather than to any king or nation. Their method is direct: close distance, hold formation, punish evil with sanctified steel. Their doctrine transforms faith from a source of comfort into a source of forward pressure — the conviction that advancing into danger in service of covenant is itself a form of prayer.

---

## I. The Sundering Wars and the Temple Desecrations

The **Sundering Wars** occupy a specific position in the world's institutional history: a conflict so politically chaotic that the traditional documentation systems of the era could not produce reliable records of who was fighting whom, for what, and under whose authority. What the clerical records of the period agree on is the **pattern of temple desecrations** — systematic violations of shrine sanctuaries by military forces who treated sacred spaces as tactically useful terrain rather than institutionally protected ground.

In the charter road system and Keeper Era framework, temples and shrines had a specific legal status: they were **neutral ground** under the Charter of Passage framework, inviolable by any party to a charter conflict because their neutrality served everyone's long-term interests (wounded soldiers on both sides needed healing access; both sides had practitioners who required shrine access for their rites). The Sundering Wars' combatants either could not enforce these protections, or chose not to.

The psychological experience of the clerics who witnessed temple desecrations — who saw the infrastructure of their practice treated as irrelevant by the people their practice was supposed to serve — produced two distinct responses. Some withdrew more deeply into the contemplative and restorative functions that would eventually become the Priest lineage: if the world would not respect the boundary between sacred and military, they would at least ensure that healing capacity survived behind that boundary. Others drew the opposite conclusion: if the boundary would not be respected, it needed to be defended, and defense required practitioners who could fight.

The high clerics who chose to fight did not do so by becoming soldiers. Soldiers serve commanders. These practitioners swore oaths to a principle — the light itself, the covenant, the maintenance of conditions under which sacred practice can occur — that overrode loyalty to any particular commander or institution. This distinction is the Paladin tradition's foundational organizational principle: **the oath is the institution**, and the institution serves no political authority above the oath's content.

## II. The Oath-Mark System

The **oath-marks** inscribed in holy silver that the tradition's earliest texts describe — said to burn cold when an enemy of the faith draws near — are not merely symbolic. The Paladin tradition maintains that the oath-marking rite, performed at the conclusion of the initial formation period, creates a genuine covenant relationship between the practitioner and the divine principle they serve. The marks are an external expression of an internal binding: the practitioner's will, formally and irrevocably aligned with the covenant's requirements.

The theological question of what "alignment with the covenant's requirements" means in practice is one the tradition addresses through case study rather than abstract doctrine. The tradition maintains an extensive archive of historical oath-mark decisions — situations where a Paladin's reading of covenant requirements conflicted with the orders of civil authorities, military commanders, or even religious superiors, and how the conflict was resolved. These case studies form the core of advanced formation training: understanding not what covenant requires in the abstract but what specific patterns of conflict arise between covenant and institutional authority, and how the tradition has historically navigated them.

The patterns that recur most often in the case study archive are: covenant requires protection of a population that political authority has ordered abandoned; covenant requires continuing an engagement that military command has ordered terminated; covenant requires refusing an order that would serve short-term tactical objectives at the cost of long-term sacred trust. In each pattern, the tradition's accumulated judgment is that the oath governs — but the archive also contains cases where Paladins exercised oath-authority against institutional directives and were, in retrospect, wrong. The tradition does not suppress these cases. They are, in the tradition's view, the most important teaching material.

## III. Shield-Wardens and the Temple Guard Lineage

The **Solar Court's temple-guard auxiliaries** are the institutional precursor that the Paladin tradition claims as an earlier ancestor than the Sundering Wars period. The Solar Court maintained wardens specifically responsible for neither pure combat nor pure ministry but both at once — practitioners who stood at the threshold between battle-clergy and ceremonial service. These auxiliaries are also claimed by the **Templar** adept path as foundational, which creates an interesting historiographical situation: both Paladin and Templar traditions claim descent from the Solar Court's temple-guard auxiliaries, though through different evolutionary paths.

The Paladin tradition's narrative of the Solar Court connection emphasizes the **oath-binding** function: the auxiliaries' commitment to the protection of sacred space, regardless of who threatened it or what tactical cost defense incurred. The Templar tradition's narrative emphasizes the **dual-function** character: the auxiliaries' capacity to perform both combat and healing functions without the specialization that the later Paladin-Priest split created. Both narratives are historically plausible and consistent with the fragmentary Solar Court records available.

The **shield-warden** role at the great temples — holding every threshold against the dark — is the Paladin tradition's self-image. The term is used within the tradition to describe the fundamental orientation that distinguishes a Paladin from a warden, knight, or soldier: the shield-warden doesn't fight to advance position or secure advantage; they fight to hold the ground that sacred practice requires. The advance-and-sanctify doctrine that characterizes modern Paladin operational style is, in the tradition's self-understanding, an extension of shield-warden logic to mobile contexts: you advance in order to extend the ground that sacred practice can safely occupy, and you sanctify as you advance so that the ground you hold is genuinely protected rather than merely defended.

## IV. The Paladin-Priest Dynamic

The **Priest tradition's** Hour of Quiet Thunder schism created the institutional context for the Paladin-Priest partnership that characterizes modern holy-path operation. Before the schism, clerics were expected to perform both combat-support and restorative functions, with varying individual emphasis but no formal specialization. After the schism, Priest practitioners concentrated in restoration while remaining clerics (now more clearly the Paladin-adjacent practitioners) concentrated in defensive advance.

The partnership that emerged is operationally effective but involves structural tension that practitioners navigate situationally. Paladins' advance creates the secure ground within which Priest practitioners can sustain healing operations; Priests' sustained healing maintains the Paladin's capacity for continued advance. The problem is that **timing** these functions requires continuous negotiation: a Paladin who advances too aggressively leaves the Priest's position exposed; a Priest who requires too long to complete healing operations constrains the Paladin's tactical options.

The solution the tradition has developed is **trust calibration** — a specific practice of pre-engagement communication between Paladin and Priest partners that establishes shared assessment of operating conditions and agreed thresholds for when the Paladin holds position and when the Priest accelerates their work. This practice is taught formally in the joint training contexts that bridge Paladin and Priest institutional cultures, and informally through accumulated operational experience between specific partnership pairs.

## V. The Sundering Wars and Modern Institutional Memory

The **Sundering Wars** are not well-documented in public archives — the conflict's institutional chaos destroyed many records, and the forces involved had varying interests in what survived. The Paladin tradition's internal archive is one of the better sources of Sundering Wars period documentation, specifically for the temple desecration events that prompted the tradition's founding.

This gives the Paladin tradition an unusual relationship to modern institutional politics. The tradition holds documentation of historical behavior by predecessor institutions of the five cities that those cities' **Continuity factions** would prefer to keep classified. Midgaard's predecessor institutions countersigned some of the sealed warrants that authorized military forces' use of sacred spaces as tactical staging grounds during the Sundering Wars. Kowloon's founding houses maintained commercial relationships with several Sundering Wars-era military factions whose desecration of specific temple sites is documented in the Paladin archive.

The Paladin tradition has not used this documentation offensively in modern political contexts — oath-holding does not extend to political leverage, and practitioners who attempted to use the archive as a political weapon would find themselves in oath-violation territory. But the documentation exists and is known to exist, and the five cities' Continuity factions are aware of it. The Paladin archive is one of several external documentation resources that constrain how aggressively Continuity factions can pursue their historical suppression objectives.
